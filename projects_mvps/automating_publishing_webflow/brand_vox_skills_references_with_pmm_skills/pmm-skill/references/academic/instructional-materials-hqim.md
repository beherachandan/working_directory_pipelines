## Core vs. Supplemental: Where Wayground Fits

### Core Curriculum (EdReports standard)
- Comprehensive standards coverage for the full year
- All knowledge, skills, and content required
- Formative and summative assessment included
- For ALL students
- Format: Textbooks, comprehensive digital programs
- Examples: Eureka Math, CKLA, Amplify, Illustrative Math, Open Sci Ed, Big Ideas, Bridges

### Supplemental Materials
- Enhances, extends, or remediates
- Usually narrower in scope
- Flexible, can be inserted as needed
- Progress monitoring and targeted assessment
- For targeted groups, differentiation
- Format: Digital (primarily), but also print

### Wayground Supports HQIM Implementation
- We are NOT HQIM. We are NOT a core curriculum.
- **We support the implementation of HQIM.** This is a critical distinction.
- When districts adopt HQIM (Eureka, IM, CKLA, Amplify, etc.), they need tools that help teachers IMPLEMENT it effectively: formative checks, practice, differentiation, progress monitoring, engagement
- Wayground is the supplemental layer that makes HQIM implementation work better: engagement, differentiation, review, extension, formative assessment
- **⚠️ NEVER position Wayground AS HQIM or as a replacement for core curriculum**
- Key insight: showing how Wayground supports/aligns to existing HQIM resolves buyer/user tension

## The Buyer vs. User Tension

### School & District Administrators (Approve the Purchase)
**What they care about:**
- Evidence of impact aligned with ESSA guidelines
- On the state "approved" list (varies by state)
- Meets procurement criteria and pricing
- Gets through the school board smoothly
- Rigor, alignment, coherence, research backing

**Their questions:**
- "Has this proven to work in a district like mine?"
- "Does it meet all criteria to make procurement easy?"
- "Will this get through the school board?"

**State-specific approved lists:**
- TX: [SBOE Approved Instructional Materials](https://tea.texas.gov/state-board-of-education/imra/imra24-list-of-sboe-approved-instructional-materials.pdf)
- OH: [Approved Core Curriculum List](https://education.ohio.gov/Topics/Learning-in-Ohio/English-Language-Art/Resources-for-English-Language-Arts/High-Quality-Instructional-Materials-in-ELA/Approved-List-of-Core-Curriculum-and-Instructional)
- FL: [Review Process Statute](http://www.leg.state.fl.us/statutes/index.cfm?App_mode=Display_Statute&Search_String=&URL=1000-1099/1006/Sections/1006.283.html)

### Specialists & Coaches (Select and Oversee)
**What they care about:**
- Standards alignment and instructional philosophy match
- Uses models and structures they believe in
- Authored or endorsed by trusted experts
- Training and support requirements for teachers
- Pacing guide adherence

**Their questions:**
- "Is this aligned to standards and our instructional philosophy?"
- "Is it endorsed by someone we trust?"
- "What training will teachers need?"

**Reference examples:**
- [SAISD pacing guide](https://www.saisd.net/page/curriculum-es-02grade)
- [CSSD11 Math philosophy](https://www.d11.org/academics/curriculum-instruction/academic-disciplines/mathematics)

### Classroom Teachers (Use Daily)
**What they care about:**
- Classroom management (misbehavior often stems from frustration)
- How to teach grade-level content when students are behind
- Relevance to students' interests and backgrounds
- Time savings
- Engagement that isn't a distraction from learning

**Their questions:**
- "How do I teach this when my kids are 2 years behind?"
- "Can I make this more relevant to my students?"
- "Will this help with classroom management?"

### The Reconciliation Strategy
**If the objective is bottoms-up enterprise purchase:**
- Buyer needs (rigor, coherence) must be embedded BY DESIGN
- But made APPROACHABLE for teachers
- Allow users to see/align to common HQIM (Eureka, Bridges, Illustrative Math, Big Ideas, Open Sci Ed, Amplify)
- **Positioning: "We support the implementation of HQIM"** — not "we are HQIM," not "we replace HQIM," not "we are a supplemental curriculum." We are the tool that helps teachers implement their adopted HQIM effectively through formative assessment, practice, differentiation, and engagement.

### ⚠️ CRITICAL POSITIONING RULE (Amber, Mar 10 2026)
**Wayground is NOT HQIM. We SUPPORT the implementation of HQIM.**
This distinction must be maintained in ALL output: briefs, talk tracks, discovery prep, email copy. Reps must have strong mastery of this pedagogical distinction. When a district has adopted Eureka Math, we don't compete with it — we make it work better by giving teachers the formative data, practice tools, and differentiation capabilities to implement it faithfully and effectively.

## Why Teachers "Go Rogue" (and Why Administrators Worry)

### The Scale of the Problem
- **90% of teachers** add, mix, and match content from the internet (RAND)
- Teachers spend **7 hours/week searching** for materials + **5 hours/week creating** their own (RAND)
- **This happens privately, without supervision**
- "The caliber, rigor, and any rational sequencing of material both within and across grade levels becomes a matter of luck and chance"

### Quality of Supplemental Materials Is Low
Fordham Institute ratings of popular supplemental platforms:

| Platform | Very Poor | Mediocre | Good | Exceptional |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Teachers Pay Teachers | 10% | 62% | 27% | 1% |
| ReadWriteThink | 9% | 46% | 39% | 6% |
| Share My Lesson | 15% | 48% | 30% | 7% |

Only 1% of TpT materials rated "Exceptional." 72% rated Mediocre or worse.

**Source:** Polikoff, M. (2019). *The Supplemental-Curriculum Bazaar: Is What's Online Any Good?* Thomas B. Fordham Institute.

### Why Teachers Supplement (Reasons)
- Saving time (their own AND instructional time)
- Relevance and engagement
- Filling gaps from previous years (students behind grade level)
- Pre-teaching or re-teaching
- Increased subject-matter expertise (especially MS/HS)
- Previous experience with materials that worked

**Teacher quote:** "I look for things that are more engaging so I can get my kids to buy in a little more. Textbooks are boring a lot of the time... I'll download fun activities for the first couple weeks of school and end-of-year stuff like review materials." (ASCD)

**Sources:**
- [ASCD: Are Your Teachers Using Subpar Curriculum Materials?](https://ascd.org/el/articles/are-your-teachers-using-subpar-curriculum-materials)
- [Hechinger Report: HS Math Teachers Cobble Together Materials](https://hechingerreport.org/proof-points-many-high-school-math-teachers-cobble-together-their-own-instructional-materials-from-the-internet-and-elsewhere-a-survey-finds/)

### What Administrators Worry About
When teachers curate/create their own materials:
- **Rigor** — Is it challenging enough?
- **Alignment** — Does it match standards?
- **Coherence** — Does it fit the sequence?
- **Research** — Is it evidence-based?
- **Appropriateness** — Is it age/culturally appropriate?
- **Accessibility** — Does it work for all learners?

## The Impact of Curriculum Quality

### Curriculum Selection Rivals Teacher Effectiveness (Brookings, 2012)

| Intervention | Effect Size (SD) |
|---|---|
| Better curriculum, 2nd-grade math | **0.17** |
| Teach for America teacher, math | 0.15 |
| 75th percentile teacher vs. average, math | 0.11 |
| 75th percentile teacher vs. average, reading | 0.08 |
| TFA teacher, reading | 0.03 |

**Key insight:** Curriculum impact is LARGEST for the weakest teachers (0.13 SD for 20th percentile vs. 0.08 for 80th percentile), effectively raising the floor. (Jackson & Makarin, NBER 2016)

**Source:** Chingos & Whitehurst, "Choosing Blindly," Brookings Institution, 2012; Jackson & Makarin, NBER Working Paper 229398, 2016.

**Study:** [Teachers' Perceptions of What Makes Instructional Materials Engaging, Appropriately Challenging, and Usable](https://www.rand.org/pubs/research_reports/RRA134-2.html)

## Grade-Level Differences in Curation

### Elementary
- Most likely to stick with prescribed curriculum
- Print resources for intervention, recognition, practice
- More hands-on/tactile learning
- SEL/classroom culture materials
- Seek support for foundational skills (Science of Reading)

### Middle School
- Students expected to be readers, but teachers lack reading support training
- Reach for non-text resources to help struggling readers access content
- Subject-focus attracts more SME
- Seek timely, real-world examples to maintain student interest

### High School
- AP/IB teachers supplement heavily for exam prep
- Project-based and student-led learning for age-appropriate autonomy
- May address sensitive topics the core curriculum avoids
- Most likely to curate from outside sources

## What Makes Materials "Good" — EdReports Framework

### EdReports (founded 2014)
- Nonprofit providing free reviews of K-12 instructional materials
- Teacher-led reviews using rigorous rubrics
- Evaluates ELA, Math, and Science for quality, alignment, usability
- Does NOT evaluate supplemental materials (beyond K-2 foundational) — but their rubrics inform what "good" looks like

**Links:**
- [EdReports Rubrics & Review Tools](https://www.edreports.org/process/review-tools)
- [EdReports FAQ](https://edreports.org/about/faqs)

### EdReports Three Pillars

**1. Focus & Coherence**
- Comprehensive standards coverage per grade
- Logical progression within and across grades
- Supports mastery over time
- Focused on grade-level work, avoids unnecessary off-grade content

**2. Rigor**
- Balances conceptual understanding, procedural skills, application
- Complex texts, challenging problems, real-world context
- Critical thinking and higher-order thinking (DOK levels, Bloom's)

**3. Usability**
- Clearly organized for the average teacher
- Teacher guidance is clear and easy to follow
- Differentiation strategies for ELs and students with disabilities
- Student guidance is age-appropriate, accessible, welcoming
- Assessments aligned and providing actionable data

### EdReports Gateway Process
Materials are evaluated in stages. Failure at any gateway stops the review:
1. **Gateway 1:** Focus & Coherence (standards coverage, progression)
2. **Gateway 2:** Rigor (balance of conceptual, procedural, application)
3. **Alignment check** between Gateway 1 and 2
4. **Gateway 3:** Usability (only evaluated if Gateways 1-2 pass)

**Lesson:** Alignment and rigor come BEFORE usability in the evaluation hierarchy. This mirrors administrator priorities.

## ELSF Guidelines for English Learners

**Source:** [ELSF Guidelines for Content Creators](https://www.elsuccessforum.org/guidelines) (Math, ELA, Science, Spanish Language Arts)

### Common Themes
- Scaffolding of skills with multiple entry points and reinforcement
- Students as active, reflective participants
- Language and content are interdependent and reinforce one another
- Being multilingual is an asset that supports learning across all content areas
- Assessments should inform both general teaching and academic language acquisition

### Recommendation
Actively consider ELSF guidelines in content creation and lesson structures. Teachers of ELs are more likely to curate and supplement, so having EL-friendly design built in reduces the need to "go rogue."

## Must-Haves vs. Delighters

### Must-Haves (Hygiene) — Required to Drive Teacher Use Into Administrator Purchase

**Academic Alignment & Rigor:**
- Comprehensive alignment visible across lessons — at minimum, cover major work of the grade
- Strong emphasis on major work of grade level
- Consistent differentiation for EL and IEP students
- Scaffolding available but not the majority of materials
- Research frameworks: DOK, Bloom's, Hattie, Marzano, Danielson
- ESSA Tier IV evidence (or better): logic model, research foundation

**Teacher Usability:**
- Consistent structure and resource type availability
- Search/filter returns manageable, relevant results
- "Grab and go" — significantly reduces cognitive load and planning time
- Doesn't require SME, but builds it intuitively
- Portability/interoperability
- Data readily available showing evidence of student learning

**Student Engagement:**
- Visuals age-appropriate but not distracting
- Supports classroom management
- Simple for students to self-serve

### Delighters — Drive Use and Evangelism

**Academic:**
- Alignment to specific core programs (Eureka, IM, etc.)
- Embedded professional learning (approachable, limits training costs)
- Support from notable thought leaders (Deborah Ball for Math, Tim Shanahan for ELA, Doug Llewellyn for Science)

**Usability:**
- Video examples/modeling — easy to copy or replicate
- Printables
- Actionable, clear data with push/pull notifications
- Customizability to time constraints

**Engagement:**
- Cultural reference/resonance
- Community/interactivity
- Hands-on opportunities
- Real-time feedback
- Home/family connection
- Opportunities to share/celebrate

**Reference:** [Kiddom's Lesson Launch](https://www.kiddom.co/kiddom-lesson-launch/) — good example of how core lessons can be adapted

## APPENDIX: Deep Dives from Embedded Sources (Crawled Mar 10 2026)

### A1. HQIM Implementation Research (Dr. David Steiner, Johns Hopkins / TNTP / RAND)

**Key findings from research:**

- **Almost 90%** of teachers add, mix, and match content from the internet (RAND). TpT is the most consulted source.
- Teachers spend **7 hours/week searching** for materials + **5 hours/week creating** their own (RAND)
- This happens **privately, without supervision** — "the caliber, rigor, and any rational sequencing of material both within and across grade levels becomes a matter of luck and chance"
- No school of education offers courses on how to meld digital resources with mandated curriculum
- TNTP found students spent **500+ hours/year on assignments not appropriate for their grade** — equivalent to 6 months of wasted class time per core subject
- **Striking equity disparity:** Classrooms serving higher-income students spent **2x as much time** on grade-appropriate assignments and **5x as much time** with strong instruction vs. low-income classrooms

**Curriculum effect sizes (Brookings, 2012):**
- Better curriculum in 2nd-grade math = **0.17 SD** effect
- Teach for America teacher in math = 0.15 SD
- 75th percentile teacher vs. average in math = 0.11 SD
- "The greatest positive impact of HQIM accrues when used by the **least experienced teachers**" (Jackson & Makarin, NBER 2016)

**Implementation reality:**
- 93% of teachers reported using the textbook for some purpose, but only **25% used it "nearly all the time"** for essential activities
- Only **7% use their curriculum exclusively**
- Up to **40%** of teachers using Eureka Math report **watering it down** when they believe it's too demanding
- Average teacher received only **1.1 days of PD** devoted to their curriculum (Blazar et al.)
- "A day and a breakfast of PD per year is unlikely to change teaching practice"

**What works for HQIM implementation:**
- Louisiana: 99% of MS/HS students have HQIM access in both ELA and math
- Tennessee: 98% of MS students have HQIM access in ELA
- Keys: more PD days, classroom observations with feedback tied to standards, alignment in evaluations, principal leadership
- "Cursory PD coupled with principals who give lip service to HQIM send powerful signals to teachers that the district is not serious about implementation"

**⚠️ Why this matters for Wayground:** We support HQIM implementation. When teachers water down Eureka or skip the district curriculum, it's often because they lack the formative data and engagement tools to make rigorous content work in their classroom. Our role: make HQIM implementation stick by giving teachers the practice, assessment, and differentiation tools that the core curriculum alone doesn't provide.

### A3. ASCD: Are Your Teachers Using Subpar Curriculum Materials? (Northern & Polikoff)

**Study:** 300+ most-downloaded materials from TpT, ReadWriteThink, Share My Lesson evaluated by ELA experts.

**Detailed quality findings:**

| Dimension | Mean (0-3 scale) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Errors/accuracy | 2.75 | Basic quality is fine |
| Organization/visual | High | Materials look professional |
| **Overall holistic rating** | **1.28** | **63% "not worth using" or "mediocre"** |
| Instructional guidance | 1.61 | Unclear teacher directions |
| Standards alignment | 1.35 | 56% only "partly aligned" |

**DOK (Depth of Knowledge) findings:**
- Most main activities were DOK 1 or 2 (recall/skills)
- Nearly **half had NO DOK level 3 content**
- Only **6%** scored above 0 for DOK level 4

**Diversity and differentiation:**
- **86% of materials scored 0** for differentiation support (no support for diverse learners)
- **68% scored 0** for cultural diversity (no inclusion of diverse perspectives)
- TpT worst on diversity (mean 0.30); Share My Lesson best (mean 0.75)

**Why teachers supplement (from interviews):**
1. Increase student engagement — "I look for things that are more engaging so I can get my kids to buy in"
2. Save time (instructional time AND prep time)
3. Fill gaps from previous years (students behind grade level)
4. Increase subject-matter expertise (especially MS/HS)

**Recommendations:**
1. Monitor the enacted curriculum — "supplementary materials may soon become part of the core curriculum whether leaders realize it or not"
2. Curate the online marketplace at district/school level
3. Look for materials with differentiation supports and stronger assessments
4. Develop repositories focused on diverse authors and cultural pluralism

### A4. Hechinger Report: HS Math Teachers Cobble Together Materials (Zahner, AERA 2024)

**Study:** 1,000+ math teachers surveyed, districts with ≥10% EL population.

**Key stats:**
- **75%+ of elementary teachers** use school-recommended materials
- **Fewer than 50% of high school teachers** use school-recommended materials
- Students taught through a "bricolage" of materials teachers assemble themselves
- Two downsides: (1) time-consuming — reduces time for feedback/tutoring, contributes to burnout; (2) sacrifices thoughtful sequencing by curriculum designers

**EL-specific findings:**
- Many teachers said existing materials **weren't connected to students' languages and cultures**
- Explanations for tailoring lessons to ELs were **too general to be useful**
- Teachers need more support in helping ELs develop mathematical reasoning language

### A5. Zahner, Skarin & Pelaez (2024): Math Materials for English Learners (Full AERA Study)

**Study:** 1,084 K-12 math teachers, nationally representative via RAND AEP.

**Strengths in materials (standards alignment):**
- 76% rated materials as teaching major topics aligned with grade-level standards (moderate/great extent)
- 68% rated materials as developing ELs' understanding of grade-level math concepts

**Weaknesses — Cultural relevance (WORST area):**
- **59% said materials did NOT adequately connect math to ELs' lived experiences and cultures**
- **63% said materials did NOT build on home languages or informal math talk**
- **"There are absolutely no relevant cultural connections made in these textbooks — not for any culture."** — 8th grade math teacher, Florida

**Weaknesses — Assessment quality (2nd worst area):**
- **55% said materials lacked consistent feedback strategies for EL math writing**
- **46% said materials lacked multiple types of formative assessment**
- **47% said materials didn't assess ELs' use of math language**

**Framework used:** ELSF's 5 Focus Areas:
1. Interdependence of content, practices, and language
2. Scaffolding for simultaneous math/language development
3. Developing mathematical rigor through language
4. **Leveraging students' assets** ← weakest
5. **Assessing content, practices, and language** ← second weakest

### A6. RAND: What's Missing from Teachers' Toolkits for Reading Grades 3-8 (Shapiro et al., 2024)

**Key findings:**
- Students spend **more than half of class time** reading and writing in grades 3-8
- Teachers estimate **44% of students** always or nearly always experience difficulty reading instructional materials
- **40% of grade 3-8 teachers** hold misconceptions about how students develop word-reading skills
- Nearly **half** of teachers say their primary knowledge of reading instruction comes from personal classroom experience (not training)
- **Three-quarters** of teachers say they need more resources to identify and support students with reading difficulties
