## The Assessment Hierarchy

Assessments exist on a spectrum from informal to formal, low-stakes to high-stakes. Understanding where each type sits helps you speak the language of whoever you're talking to.

```
Universal Screener → Diagnostic → Formative → Benchmark → Interim → Summative
   (who needs help?)  (what specifically?)  (are they learning?)  (on track?)  (checkpoint)  (did they learn it?)
```

### Universal Screener
**What it is:** A brief assessment given to ALL students (typically 3x/year: fall, winter, spring) to identify who is at risk and needs intervention.

**Examples:** MAP (NWEA), iReady (Curriculum Associates), DIBELS, AIMSweb, Star (Renaissance), FastBridge

**Key characteristics:**
- Short (15-30 min typically)
- Norm-referenced (compares student to national sample)
- Adaptive (adjusts difficulty based on responses)
- Administered universally (every student, no exceptions)
- Results drive tier placement in RTI/MTSS frameworks

**What districts care about:** Speed, accuracy of risk identification, false positive/negative rates, integration with intervention platforms

**Wayground connection:** We are NOT a screener and should never position as one. We complement screeners by providing the ongoing formative data between screening windows. When a screener says "this student is at risk," Wayground helps the teacher monitor whether interventions are working day-to-day.

---

### Formative Assessment
**What it is:** Ongoing, low-stakes assessment during instruction to check understanding and adjust teaching in real time.

**Key characteristics:**
- Embedded in instruction (not a separate "testing event")
- Low or no stakes (doesn't count toward grades in most implementations)
- Immediate feedback loop: data → teacher adjusts → student improves
- Teacher-created or teacher-selected (aligned to what they're actually teaching)
- Frequent (daily, weekly)

**The critical distinction:** Formative assessment isn't just about testing. It's about the feedback loop. A quiz with no action taken on the results is just a quiz. Formative assessment means the data changes what happens next in the classroom.

**Wayground connection:** This is our core. Live games, homework, practice modes, and CA are all formative tools. Our differentiator: the feedback is instant (real-time dashboards during games), actionable (teachers see exactly which standards students missed), and engaging (students actually complete the work because it's not boring).

---

### Benchmark Assessment
**What it is:** Assessment given at set intervals (quarterly, trimester, or by unit) to measure progress toward grade-level standards.

**Key characteristics:**
- Scheduled at regular intervals (not daily like formative)
- Standards-aligned (maps to specific grade-level expectations)
- Often district-mandated (all teachers in a grade/subject give the same one)
- Results used for instructional planning and predicting summative performance
- Can be teacher-created or purchased (from vendors like Wayground, Performance Matters, MasteryConnect)

**Wayground connection:** CA (Common Assessments) can serve a benchmark function when districts schedule them at regular intervals across schools. The difference: our benchmarks are built by teachers (or from our item bank), given through an engaging platform, and produce instant results. Traditional benchmarks often take days/weeks to score and return data.

---

### Interim Assessment
**What it is:** Mid-cycle assessment that sits between benchmark and summative. Often designed to mimic the format of the state test.

**Key characteristics:**
- Typically 2-3 per year
- State-test format (same question types, similar length)
- Predictive (used to estimate how students will perform on the state test)
- Often purchased from the state or a testing vendor

**Wayground connection:** We don't typically play in the interim space directly, but CA data combined with our standards-alignment reporting gives districts a formative alternative that's more frequent and actionable than 2-3 interims per year.

---

### Summative Assessment
**What it is:** End-of-unit or end-of-year assessment evaluating cumulative learning. High-stakes.

**Examples:** State tests (STAAR, SBAC, PARCC, Regents, EOC exams), AP exams, final exams, SAT/ACT

**Key characteristics:**
- High stakes (affects school ratings, teacher evaluations, student promotion/graduation)
- Standardized (same test, same conditions, same scoring)
- Administered once (or few times) per year
- Results come weeks/months later
- Used for accountability, not instructional adjustment

**Wayground connection:** We are NOT a summative platform. We're the formative practice and assessment layer that helps students prepare for summatives. Our pitch: the more formative data teachers act on throughout the year, the better students perform on summatives. We fill the 170+ school days between state tests with actionable data.

## Psychometric Concepts

These terms come up when talking to assessment directors, C&I leads, and anyone evaluating assessment tools. Knowing them builds credibility.

### Validity
**Definition:** Does the test measure what it's supposed to measure?

**Types:**
- **Content validity** — Items align to the standards/curriculum being assessed. (Do the questions match what was taught?)
- **Construct validity** — The test measures the intended skill or knowledge. (Does a "reading comprehension" test actually measure comprehension, or just vocabulary?)
- **Criterion validity** — Test scores predict or correlate with other measures. (Do high scores on our benchmark predict high scores on the state test?)
- **Face validity** — Does it LOOK like a legitimate assessment to stakeholders? (Less scientific, but matters politically.)

**Why it matters in sales:** Assessment directors will ask about alignment to state standards. That's a content validity question. Our answer: teacher-created assessments aligned to what they're actually teaching (high content validity), plus a standards-tagged item bank reviewed for alignment.

### Reliability
**Definition:** Does the test produce consistent results? If a student takes it twice (or two equivalent forms), do they score similarly?

**Measures:**
- **Cronbach's alpha** — Internal consistency (do items on the same test measure the same thing?). Large standardized tests target 0.85+.
- **Test-retest reliability** — Same student, same test, different day. Consistent?
- **Inter-rater reliability** — Different graders, same response. Same score? (Relevant for open-ended/constructed response items.)
- **Parallel forms reliability** — Two different versions of the same test produce similar scores.

**Why it matters in sales:** Formative assessments don't need the same reliability thresholds as summative tests. A 5-question exit ticket doesn't need a 0.90 Cronbach's alpha. What matters is that the data is actionable and directionally accurate. If a rep is talking to someone who asks about reliability, acknowledge the distinction: "Our assessments are designed for instructional decision-making, not classification decisions. The reliability standard for 'should I reteach fractions tomorrow?' is different from 'should this student be retained?'"

### Item Response Theory (IRT)
**Definition:** A statistical framework for designing and scoring tests. Models the probability of a correct response based on:
- **Student ability** (theta) — estimated from response patterns
- **Item difficulty** (b parameter) — how hard the question is
- **Item discrimination** (a parameter) — how well the item separates high-ability from low-ability students
- **Guessing parameter** (c parameter) — probability of getting it right by chance

**Used by:** MAP/NWEA, iReady, Star, most adaptive assessments. IRT enables computer-adaptive testing (CAT) where the test adjusts difficulty in real time.

**Wayground connection:** We don't use IRT models. We provide classical test theory metrics (item difficulty, discrimination index) in our analytics. This is appropriate for classroom-level formative assessment. If someone asks about IRT, the honest answer: "IRT is designed for large-scale standardized assessments with thousands of students per item. For classroom formatives with 25-150 students, classical metrics are more appropriate and more interpretable for teachers."

### Classical Test Theory (CTT)
**Definition:** The simpler, older approach to test analysis. Looks at:
- **Item difficulty** — % of students who got it right (p-value). Easy item = high p-value.
- **Item discrimination** — How well the item separates students who know the material from those who don't. Positive discrimination = good item.
- **Distractor analysis** — For multiple choice: are the wrong answers actually attracting students with misconceptions, or are they obviously wrong?

**Wayground connection:** Our item-level analytics (in CA and standard game reports) use CTT concepts. Teachers can see which questions were too easy, too hard, or not discriminating well. This is practical, actionable analytics that helps teachers improve their assessments over time.

### Standards Alignment
**Definition:** The degree to which assessment items map to specific learning standards (state standards, Common Core, NGSS, etc.).

**Why it matters:** Districts require it. Period. If a tool can't show which standard each item assesses, it's a non-starter for purchased assessment products.

**Wayground connection:** Our content library is standards-tagged. Teacher-created content can be tagged to standards. CA reporting breaks down performance by standard. This is table stakes, but worth mentioning because some engagement-first competitors (Kahoot, Gimkit) don't have standards alignment at all.

## Assessment Vocabulary Quick Reference

Use these naturally in conversation. They signal you understand the buyer's world.

| Term | What It Means | When You'll Hear It |
|---|---|---|
| DOK (Depth of Knowledge) | Webb's framework: 4 levels from recall to extended thinking | Curriculum discussions, item quality reviews |
| Bloom's Taxonomy | 6 cognitive levels: remember → create | Lesson planning, assessment design conversations |
| Item bank | Repository of pre-built, vetted assessment questions | CA and benchmark discussions |
| Standards mastery | Student demonstrated proficiency on a specific standard | Reporting and data discussions |
| Cut score | The score that separates proficient from not proficient | State test and benchmark discussions |
| Accommodations | Test modifications for students with IEPs/504s/ELLs | Special education and equity discussions |
| TEI (Technology-Enhanced Item) | Interactive digital question types beyond multiple choice | Assessment modernization discussions |
| Constructed response | Open-ended questions requiring written answers | Assessment rigor discussions |
| Selected response | Multiple choice, matching, true/false | Item type discussions |
| Performance task | Extended activity demonstrating applied knowledge | Deeper learning and portfolio discussions |
| Norm-referenced | Compares student to peers (percentiles) | Screener and diagnostic discussions |
| Criterion-referenced | Measures against a fixed standard (proficient/not) | Standards-based grading discussions |
| Growth model | Measures how much a student improved over time | MAP, Star, district data discussions |
| Proficiency model | Measures whether a student met the standard (yes/no) | State test, accountability discussions |
