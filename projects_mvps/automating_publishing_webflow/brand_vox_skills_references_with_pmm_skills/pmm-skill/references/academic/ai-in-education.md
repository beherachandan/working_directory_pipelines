## The Landscape

AI in K-12 is evolving fast. Districts are in one of three postures:
1. **Cautious adoption** — "We're exploring, but carefully." Most common. Policies being written.
2. **Active adoption** — "We've approved specific tools and use cases." Growing, especially in forward-leaning districts.
3. **Restrictive** — "We've blocked AI tools until we figure this out." Shrinking, but still present.

Wayground needs to be credible in ALL three conversations.

## AI Literacy Frameworks

### What Districts Are Thinking About
- **For students:** Understanding what AI is, how it works, when to use it, how to evaluate AI output, ethics/bias awareness
- **For teachers:** Using AI tools effectively for instruction and assessment, understanding limitations, maintaining academic integrity
- **For administrators:** Policy development, vendor vetting, data privacy, equitable access

### Key Frameworks
- **ISTE (International Society for Technology in Education):** Published AI guidance for educators. Emphasizes: AI as a tool, not a replacement; human oversight; equity considerations; data privacy.
- **TeachAI:** Coalition (ISTE + ETS + Khan Academy + others) developing K-12 AI guidance. Focus: AI literacy standards, responsible use frameworks.
- **UNESCO:** Global AI in education guidelines. Less relevant for US K-12 sales but occasionally referenced by policy-oriented districts.
- **State-level policies:** California, Virginia, Oregon, others have issued AI guidance for schools. Vary widely.

### Wayground Connection
Our AI features (AuraGen for content creation, AI-enhanced accommodations, AI-powered analytics) are designed as teacher tools, not student-facing AI. This distinction matters:
- **Teacher uses AI to create** → saves time, maintains teacher agency
- **Student uses AI to cheat** → the fear every district has

Our positioning: AI amplifies teacher expertise. The teacher decides what to teach, how to assess, what accommodations to offer. AI handles the tedious parts (generating question variants, suggesting accommodations, analyzing performance patterns).

## District AI Policies

### What They Typically Cover
1. **Approved tools list** — Which AI tools are sanctioned? (Wayground needs to be on this list)
2. **Acceptable use** — What AI can be used for? (Content creation yes, grading maybe, student-facing chatbot probably not)
3. **Data privacy** — Where does student data go? Is it used to train models? (Critical for vendor vetting)
4. **Academic integrity** — How do we prevent AI-assisted cheating?
5. **Equity** — Do all students have equal access to AI tools?
6. **Professional development** — How are teachers being trained?

### What This Means for Sales
When a district has an AI policy, they've thought carefully about AI. They'll ask:
- "Does student data leave your platform?" → Answer: explain our data practices
- "Is student data used to train AI models?" → Know our answer to this (critical)
- "How does your AI content creation work?" → AuraGen explanation: teacher-controlled, teacher-reviewed, standards-aligned
- "What guardrails exist?" → Teacher approval workflow, content review, standards alignment checks

**Districts WITHOUT an AI policy** are either:
- Small/rural and haven't gotten to it yet (approach with education, not pressure)
- Actively developing one (offer to be a resource — thought partner mode)

## AI in Assessment — The Nuanced Conversation

### The Concern
Districts worry about:
- AI generating assessment items that aren't valid/reliable
- AI grading replacing teacher judgment
- Students using AI to cheat on assessments
- AI bias in question generation or scoring

### Our Position
1. **AI for creation, human for judgment:** AuraGen helps teachers create content faster. Teachers review, edit, and approve everything before students see it. AI is the assistant, teacher is the author.
2. **Assessment integrity:** Test mode with lockdown browser for CA. This is about protecting the assessment environment, not about AI detection (which is unreliable).
3. **Standards alignment:** AI-generated content is tagged to standards. Teachers verify alignment. The AI suggests, the teacher decides.
4. **Transparency:** When AI is used in content creation, it's a tool the teacher controls, not an autonomous system making decisions about students.

### What Competitors Are Doing
- **MagicSchool AI:** Positioning as THE AI tool for teachers. Broad AI assistant (lesson plans, rubrics, IEPs, emails). Not assessment-focused. Risk: does everything, masters nothing.
- **Khan Academy (Khanmigo):** AI tutor for students. Different category — student-facing AI. Raises different questions (dependency, accuracy, equity of access).
- **Formative:** Added some AI features for item generation. Limited scope.
- **Most CA competitors:** Haven't meaningfully integrated AI yet. This is a differentiation opportunity.

## Responsible AI Talking Points

Use these when the conversation turns to AI concerns:

| Concern | Response Approach |
|---|---|
| "AI will replace teachers" | "Every AI feature we build puts the teacher in control. AI creates draft content. The teacher reviews, edits, approves. AI analyzes data. The teacher decides what to do with it. We're building tools that give teachers superpowers, not replacements." |
| "What about bias?" | "AI-generated content goes through teacher review. Standards alignment provides a structural check. We take this seriously and it's why teacher oversight is baked into every workflow, not optional." |
| "Student data and AI" | Know and state clearly: what data is used, where it goes, whether it trains models. If you don't know the specifics, say "Let me get you the exact answer from our team" rather than guessing. |
| "Our board hasn't approved AI tools" | "That's smart governance. Would it help if I shared how other districts have approached AI policy development? We're happy to be a resource, no sales pitch needed." (Thought partner mode.) |
| "How do we prevent AI cheating?" | "Two angles: our test mode with lockdown browser protects assessment integrity during testing. And our formative approach means low-stakes practice is frequent enough that cheating on any single instance doesn't matter — the data signal comes from patterns over time, not one test." |

## Key Acronyms and Terms

| Term | Definition | Context |
|---|---|---|
| GenAI | Generative AI (ChatGPT, Claude, etc.) | The category most districts are grappling with |
| LLM | Large Language Model | The technology behind GenAI tools |
| AI literacy | Understanding what AI is, how it works, its limitations | Emerging as a curriculum topic |
| Algorithmic bias | AI systems reflecting or amplifying societal biases | Equity concern in assessment |
| Explainability | Can the AI explain why it made a decision? | Important for assessment/grading contexts |
| Human-in-the-loop | AI assists, human decides | Our model for AuraGen and all AI features |
| FERPA | Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act | Governs student data — AI tools must comply |
| COPPA | Children's Online Privacy Protection Act | Governs data for children under 13 |
| Student Data Privacy Consortium (SDPC) | Organization managing data privacy agreements | Many districts require SDPC agreements for vendors |
