## What Is UDL?

Universal Design for Learning is an educational framework developed by CAST, based on scientific insights into how humans learn. The core idea: design instruction that works for ALL learners from the start, rather than retrofitting accommodations after the fact.

UDL is supported by neuroscience research on three brain networks:
- **Affective networks** (the WHY) — How learners get engaged and stay motivated
- **Recognition networks** (the WHAT) — How learners perceive and comprehend information
- **Strategic networks** (the HOW) — How learners plan, execute, and monitor actions

## The Three Principles

### 1. Multiple Means of Engagement (the WHY of learning)
How do you motivate students and sustain their interest?

**Guidelines:**
- Recruit interest — Optimize individual choice and autonomy; optimize relevance, value, and authenticity; minimize threats and distractions
- Sustain effort and persistence — Heighten salience of goals and objectives; vary demands and resources; foster collaboration and community; increase mastery-oriented feedback
- Self-regulation — Promote expectations and beliefs that optimize motivation; facilitate personal coping skills and strategies; develop self-assessment and reflection

**Wayground alignment:** Gamification (choice of modes, avatars, leaderboards), multiple practice formats (live, self-paced, homework), collaborative and competitive options, real-time feedback loops.

### 2. Multiple Means of Representation (the WHAT of learning)
How do you present information in different ways?

**Guidelines:**
- Perception — Offer ways of customizing the display of information; offer alternatives for auditory information; offer alternatives for visual information
- Language and symbols — Clarify vocabulary and symbols; clarify syntax and structure; support decoding of text, math notation, and symbols; promote understanding across languages; illustrate through multiple media
- Comprehension — Activate or supply background knowledge; highlight patterns, critical features, big ideas, and relationships; guide information processing and visualization; maximize transfer and generalization

**Wayground alignment:** 25+ question types (text, image, audio, video, drag-and-drop), interactive video, lessons with embedded media, read-aloud accommodations, immersive reader, multilingual support.

### 3. Multiple Means of Action & Expression (the HOW of learning)
How do students demonstrate what they know?

**Guidelines:**
- Physical action — Vary the methods for response and navigation; optimize access to tools and assistive technologies
- Expression and communication — Use multiple media for communication; use multiple tools for construction and composition; build fluencies with graduated levels of support for practice and performance
- Executive functions — Guide appropriate goal-setting; support planning and strategy development; facilitate managing information and resources; enhance capacity for monitoring progress

**Wayground alignment:** Multiple response types (selected response, constructed response, draw, drag-and-drop), self-paced and teacher-led modes, mastery mode for graduated practice, standards-level progress tracking.

## UDL Guidelines 3.0 — What's New (July 2024)

Key expansions from the latest version:

1. **Identity as part of variability.** Previous versions emphasized cognitive/learning variability. 3.0 adds learners' multiple and intersecting identities (race, culture, language, disability, gender) as a dimension woven across all three principles. The "who" of learning alongside the why, what, and how.

2. **Intersection with culturally sustaining pedagogies.** UDL 3.0 explicitly connects with asset-based approaches that center, value, and sustain learners' cultural practices. This isn't just about access anymore — it's about whose knowledge and ways of knowing are valued.

3. **Addressing systemic barriers.** Acknowledges that barriers exist at individual, institutional, and system levels. Not just "how do I design my lesson better" but "what structures in our system exclude certain learners?"

4. **Interdependence and collective learning.** Shifts from purely individual learning to valuing collaborative, collective knowledge-building.

5. **Learner-centered language.** Guidelines use verbs that work for both educators AND learners, emphasizing shared agency. The educator designs for agency; the learner exercises it.

6. **Joy and play.** Explicitly promotes the role of joy and play for both learners AND educators.

7. **Restorative practices.** Includes cultivating empathy and repairing harm — connecting UDL to behavioral/SEL frameworks.

## Why UDL Matters for Sales Conversations

**When you hear "UDL" from a buyer, they're signaling:**
- They think systematically about inclusive design (sophisticated buyer)
- They want tools that DON'T require separate accommodations bolted on
- They care about equity and access (not just compliance)
- They likely value student agency and choice

**Our strongest UDL talking point:**
"Wayground is UDL by design. 25+ accommodations are built into every activity, not a separate module. Every student can have a different experience in the same assignment — extended time, read-aloud, reduced answer choices, immersive reader — without the teacher creating multiple versions. That's what UDL looks like in practice."

**UDL 3.0 talking point:**
"UDL 3.0 emphasizes that engagement should center students' identities and cultural practices. Our multilingual support, culturally diverse content library, and student-choice features (avatars, themes, modes) let students see themselves in the platform. It's not just accessible — it's affirming."
