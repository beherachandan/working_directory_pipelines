# 1-to-1 Admin Sales Email Style Guide
*Source: Cody's PMM/Enablement folder • March 2026*

## Core Principles
- **Human, not commercial:** Write like a trusted colleague, not a marketing department
- **Brief and focused:** Respect their time with concise communication
- **Clear and direct:** One obvious action or takeaway per email
- **Confidence without arrogance:** Sound assured but not pushy
- **Authentic and conversational:** Match how professionals actually communicate

## Voice & Tone

### DO:
- Write in natural, conversational style
- Use contractions (we're, you're, I've)
- Vary sentence length to create rhythm
- Address pain points directly
- Use personal pronouns (I, we, you)
- Reference specific details about their organization when possible

### DON'T:
- Use marketing jargon or buzzwords
- Include unnecessary exclamation points
- Add fluff or filler content
- Sound overly formal or stiff
- Use excessive adverbs or adjectives
- Include meaningless pleasantries ("I hope this email finds you well")

## Email Structure

### Subject Lines
- 5-7 words maximum
- Keep under 35 characters
- Specific and relevant to recipient
- Include their name or company name when possible
- Avoid clickbait or ALL CAPS
- Convey value or reason for contact

### Opening Lines
- Get straight to the point
- Avoid cliché openings
- Reference a specific pain point or opportunity
- Connect to their context or role
- Skip unnecessary introductions in follow-ups

### Body Content
- One clear message or purpose
- 2-3 short paragraphs maximum
- Each paragraph 1-3 sentences
- Bullet points sparingly for quick scanning
- Focus on "you" not "we/our product"

### Call to Action
- One clear, low-commitment next step
- Specific time options (not "when works for you")
- Frame as helping them, not requesting their time
- Make responding easy with simple questions
- Include your availability in initial outreach

## Language Patterns

### Power Words
- **Problem-solution:** resolved, addressed, overcame
- **Results-focused:** achieved, improved, increased
- **Collaboration:** partnership, together, alignment
- **Specificity:** specifically, precisely, exactly
- **Decision-making:** considered, evaluated, determined

### Phrases to AVOID
- "Feel free to..."
- "Just checking in..."
- "I wanted to..."
- "As per my last email..."
- "To be honest..."
- "I hope this email finds you well..."
- "Just following up..."
- "Sorry to bother you..."
- "I'm not sure if..."

### Confidence Boosters
- "I think" → "I'm confident"
- "we might be able to" → "we can"
- "could potentially" → "will"
- "maybe we should" → "let's"
- "I feel like" → "I've observed"

## Email Sequence Best Practices
1. **Initial Email:** Clear value proposition and specific CTA
2. **Follow-Up 1:** New information or insight, reference previous email
3. **Follow-Up 2:** Different angle on value, address potential objection
4. **Follow-Up 3:** Case study or social proof
5. **Break-Up Email:** Give permission to end sequence if no interest

## Examples

### Strong Example:
**Subject:** [org] + Quizizz: 3 min conversation?

Hi [Name],

Your recent focus on differentiation caught my attention, especially with your 30% ELL population.

Three middle schools in your area use Quizizz to create a single resource that automatically adjusts to each student's needs. Their teachers save time planning while seeing improvement in assessment scores.

Would a 15-minute conversation next Tuesday or Wednesday afternoon make sense to explore if similar results are possible for [org]?

— Jackson

### Weak Example (what NOT to do):
**Subject:** Exciting EdTech Solution for Your School!

Hi there,

I hope this email finds you well! I wanted to reach out because our revolutionary platform is transforming education across the country with our game-changing instructional suite!

*(Too vague, over-promises, marketing jargon, no specific benefit)*

## The Final Test
Before sending, ask yourself:
1. Would I respond to this email if I received it?
2. Is there a clear reason why this matters to the recipient?
3. Have I made it easy for them to say yes or no?
4. Does this sound like it was written by a human for a human?
5. Could I read this out loud without it sounding awkward?

**Human Connection > Perfect Polish**
