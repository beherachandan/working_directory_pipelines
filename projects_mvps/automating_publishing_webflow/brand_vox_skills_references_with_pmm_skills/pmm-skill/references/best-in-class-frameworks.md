# Best-in-Class PMM Frameworks
## Synthesized from Klue, Gong, and Lavender methodologies
## Applied to Wayground context (Mar 7 2026)

---

## 1. KLUE: Deal-First Competitive Enablement

### Core Principle
Competitive Intelligence is nothing without Competitive Enablement. Collecting intel is worthless if it doesn't reach the right person at the right time in the right deal.

### The Klue Model (adapted for us)
1. **Detect** — When a competitor is mentioned in a call or CRM, flag it
2. **Contextualize** — What stage is this deal? What's the buyer's priority? What specifically did they say about the competitor?
3. **Recommend** — Cross-reference against our competitive playbook → deliver stage-specific, deal-specific guidance
4. **Enable** — The output isn't a battlecard document. It's a Deal Tip: "In your Tomball call tomorrow, Tina mentioned they're evaluating assessment tools. Here's how to position CA against MasteryConnect based on what their assessment team cares about."

### Win-Loss Integration
- Win-loss insights flow INTO positioning, not sit in a separate report
- Every lost deal teaches positioning. Every won deal validates it.
- Our deal journeys (22 traced) ARE win-loss data — Robeson (won) and Madison Metro (lost) are positioning lessons

### What This Means for Me
When someone says "I have a call with [district]" — I don't give a static battlecard. I pull:
- Their deal journey so far (which calls, which stage, who's involved)
- Competitor mentions from their prior calls
- Stage-specific talk track from the playbook
- Similar deals at similar stages (won AND lost) for pattern-matching
- And generate a Deal Tip: specific, actionable, grounded in evidence

## 2. GONG: Conversation Intelligence (Data from 2M+ calls)

### Talk-to-Listen Ratio by Stage
| Stage | Optimal Ratio | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Cold call/outbound | Talk MORE (55-65%) | Grab attention. If prospect talks too much on cold call, you lose them. |
| Discovery | Listen MORE (prospect 60-70%) | Build trust. Understand needs. |
| Demo | Balanced (50-50) | Show relevant features, check in frequently |
| Proposal | Listen MORE | Understand concerns, budget reality |

### How Top Reps Compare to Our Data
- Gong: Top reps cover 15 topics (avg: 12) on 43-min calls, clustered thematically
- Our data: Haywood/Dan Gross cover instructional goals → PLCs → coaches → product in linear flow. Mark covers weather → personal → product in disjointed flow.
- Gong: Successful calls are 2x more interactive (more back-and-forth)
- Our data: Dan Gross's calls have high interactivity (prospects asking questions throughout). Mark's demos tend to be one-directional.

### The 11-14 Question Rule
Gong found: 11-14 discovery questions about 3-4 problem areas is optimal. More feels like interrogation. Fewer misses critical info.
- Haywood's structure hits this naturally: 3-4 areas (instructional goals, PLCs/coaches, current tools, evaluation process) with 3-4 questions each
- April's calls tend to under-ask (2-3 questions, heavy rapport instead)

### Opening Structure (data-backed)
1. Small talk (2-3 min) — warm the tone
2. Propose agenda — "Here's what I'd like to cover. What would you add?"
3. Upfront contract — "By the end, you'll either want to explore further or cleanly part ways."
- Haywood does steps 1-2 naturally ("I have you for 30 min, here's a brief agenda")
- Missing: upfront contract. None of our reps set the next-step expectation up front. Coaching opportunity.

### Language Transition
- "We" language in first 4-6 min (company credibility)
- "I" language after 5 min (personal rapport)
- Don't mirror negative language — stay positive until prospect aligns
- Our observation: Dan Gross naturally stays positive even when prospects are frustrated. Mark tends to empathize by matching negative energy ("yeah, that's tough").

### Key Insight for Our PMM
**Afternoon calls have 30% fewer no-shows.** For districts in different time zones, scheduling afternoon local time = higher show rate. Simple but impactful.

## 3. LAVENDER: Empathy-First Communication

### Core Framework: The "So What?" Test
Every sentence in outbound communication must pass: "Why does the reader care?"
- ❌ "Wayground has 25+ accommodations" → So what?
- ✅ "Your IEP coordinator won't need to re-enter the same accommodations across 6 different platforms anymore" → Teacher's pain solved.

### The Empathy Hierarchy
1. **Acknowledge their situation** — Show you understand what they're living with
2. **Validate the feeling** — Don't skip to the solution
3. **Connect to their priority** — Link your value to what THEY care about, not what you want to sell
4. **Offer the path** — Only now introduce the solution

Example applied to our context:
1. "Managing 6 different EdTech platforms for one district is exhausting" (acknowledge)
2. "Especially when each one has its own login, its own training, its own data dashboard" (validate)
3. "Your teachers need to spend their time teaching, not navigating tools" (connect)
4. "What if one platform replaced three of those, with all the data in one place?" (offer)

### Personalization > Generalization
- Generic: "Wayground helps districts with assessment and engagement"
- Personalized: "Your district has 149 teachers already using Wayground for free. Here's what they're doing with it."
- Our application: Pre-call briefs should include specific usage data for THAT district. We have this data in BigQuery.

### Clarity Rules
- Short sentences. One idea per sentence.
- No jargon unless the prospect uses it first (they say "formative assessment" → you can say it back)
- If a teacher wouldn't say it in a staff meeting, don't write it in a sales email
- Read it aloud. If it sounds like a vendor wrote it, rewrite it.

## 4. SYNTHESIS: My Operating System

### Before Any PMM Output, Check:

**Empathy checkpoint (Carrie's feedback + Lavender):**
- [ ] Does this lead with the teacher's frustration, not our feature?
- [ ] Would a teacher recognize themselves in this language?
- [ ] Does every sentence pass the "so what?" test?

**Stage checkpoint (Gong + Klue):**
- [ ] What deal stage is this for?
- [ ] Am I recommending the right behavior for this stage?
- [ ] Does my talk-to-listen guidance match the stage?

**Deal-first checkpoint (Klue):**
- [ ] Is this specific to THIS deal, or is it generic?
- [ ] Have I referenced what the prospect actually said?
- [ ] Am I connecting this to a similar deal we've seen (won or lost)?

**Positioning checkpoint (Carrie's feedback):**
- [ ] Am I leading with displacement intelligence, not competitor history?
- [ ] Am I using prospect language, not vendor language?
- [ ] Have I referenced roadmap/unreleased features? (If yes, DELETE)

### Quality Bar
If I can't anchor a positioning statement to either:
1. A real teacher quote from a transcript, OR
2. A real deal outcome (won/lost) from our data

Then it doesn't go in. Theory without evidence is just marketing fluff.
