# Persona: District Superintendent

## Role & Title
- **Title:** Superintendent of Schools
- **Level:** C-suite equivalent for K-12 districts
- **Scope:** District-wide decisions, board accountability, community relations

## Primary Concerns
1. **District-wide outcomes** — test scores, graduation rates, equity metrics
2. **Budget stewardship** — justifying spend to the school board and taxpayers
3. **Teacher retention & satisfaction** — reducing burnout, supporting staff
4. **Compliance & accountability** — state standards, federal mandates, reporting
5. **Community perception** — board meetings, parent satisfaction, public trust

## Language They Use
- "District-wide initiative" / "systemic implementation"
- "Return on investment" / "cost per student"
- "Board priorities" / "strategic plan alignment"
- "Equity" / "access for all students"
- "Data-driven decision making"
- "Scalable" / "sustainable"

## Decision Authority
- **Can approve:** District-wide contracts, multi-year agreements, 6-figure+ deals
- **Needs escalation:** Board approval for large expenditures (threshold varies by district)
- **Delegates to:** Curriculum director (pedagogy evaluation), CTO/IT director (technical review)
- **Influenced by:** Board priorities, teacher requests, peer superintendent recommendations

## Objection Patterns
- "How does this align with our strategic plan?"
- "What's the total cost of ownership across the district?"
- "Show me outcomes data from similar districts"
- "We need to run a pilot first"
- "How does this address our equity goals?"

## Win Triggers
- **Board alignment:** Their board is already discussing topics that map to Wayground's value props
- **Teacher groundswell:** Multiple teachers already using free version and requesting paid (PQA signal)
- **Peer validation:** Neighboring district or state peer already using Wayground successfully
- **Data story:** Clear data showing ROI — cost per teacher, time savings, student engagement lift
- **Timing:** Budget cycle alignment (Q2-Q3 for next school year planning)

## Data Points That Matter
Lead with district-level metrics:
- **Total active teachers** in district (from `clean.game` + `clean.user`)
- **District-wide engagement** — total games, students reached
- **Growth trajectory** — month-over-month teacher adoption curve
- **Board meeting alignment** — which board priorities map to Wayground (from `account_feature_store`)
- **Comparable district data** — similar-sized districts using Wayground
- **Cost per teacher** — ACV ÷ active teachers for ROI framing

## Problem Statements (from their perspective)
- "We're paying for 12 different platforms that do the same thing"
- "I can't justify another subscription when I can't prove ROI"
- "Achievement gaps are widening despite our technology investments"
- "We invested heavily, but only 30% of teachers actually use it"
- "Teachers are burning out from technology fatigue"
- "Free tools are costing us more in support time than paid solutions"

## How to Engage
- Start with board priorities (from board meeting data)
- Frame everything as district-wide, not individual teacher
- Lead with outcomes and ROI, not features
- Reference their strategic plan themes
- Provide peer district references
- Always have a "board-ready" summary available

## Pillar Routing
- **Primary:** MEASURE (outcomes, ROI, board-ready data)
- **Secondary:** PLAN (consolidation, budget stewardship)
- **When they say:** "Show me outcomes from similar districts" → Lead with MEASURE
- **When they say:** "What's the total cost of ownership?" → Lead with PLAN (consolidation)

## Hidden Pain: Three-Layer Tool Sprawl (Carrie, Mar 9 2026)
Superintendents see their 2 district-level vendor contracts and think tool management is handled. But tool adoption happens at three layers they can't all see:
1. **District purchases** (visible, budgeted) — the 2 contracts they manage
2. **School purchases** (semi-visible) — principals buying point solutions for their building
3. **Teacher free tools** (invisible) — individual teachers using Kahoot, Blooket, Quizlet free tiers

The superintendent's real problem: no unified data, no consistent student experience, no visibility into what's actually happening in classrooms. This is a high-value discovery question: "Beyond your two district contracts, do you know how many other tools are running in your classrooms right now?"
