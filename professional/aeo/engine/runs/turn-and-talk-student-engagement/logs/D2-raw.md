### CLAIM 2 — Mary Budd Rowe, wait time

> "According to Mary Budd Rowe's landmark research on wait time — 'Wait Time: Slowing Down May Be a Way of Speeding Up!' (*Journal of Teacher Education*, 37(1), 1986) — students who receive three or more seconds of processing time before speaking produce longer, more accurate, and more elaborated answers than students given less than one second."

**Status: ✅ Verified (with one minor precision note)**

- **Article title:** "Wait Time: Slowing Down May Be a Way of Speeding Up!" — correct. This is the title of Rowe's 1986 retrospective piece.
- **Journal:** *Journal of Teacher Education* — correct journal for this piece.
- **Volume/issue:** 37(1) — consistent with the 1986 publication record.
- **Year:** 1986 — correct.
- **Claim about outcomes:** Rowe's research found that extending teacher wait time from the average of ~1 second to 3–5 seconds produced longer student responses, increased confidence, more speculative thinking, and fewer failures to respond. The article's characterization ("longer, more accurate, and more elaborated") is accurate in direction and substance.

**Precision note:** Rowe's original foundational work on wait time was published in the 1970s (Science Education, 1974). The 1986 *JTE* article is a retrospective synthesis, not the original study. This is acceptable framing for a practitioner article (it cites a real, accessible piece), but a technically precise citation might reference the original 1974 work. No correction required; no distortion present.

### CLAIM 6 — John Hattie, Visible Learning effect sizes

> "According to John Hattie's *Visible Learning* synthesis of more than 800 meta-analyses (*Visible Learning for Teachers*, 2012)… effect sizes ranging from d = 0.50 to d = 0.82 depending on implementation quality… Hattie identifies as the threshold separating typical from high-impact instruction."

**Status: ⚠️ Needs Confirmation — effect size range precision**

- **Book title:** *Visible Learning for Teachers* (2012) — correct. The original *Visible Learning* was 2009; the 2012 *for Teachers* volume is a legitimate follow-on.
- **"800+ meta-analyses":** The original 2009 *Visible Learning* synthesized approximately 800 meta-analyses. The 2012 volume extended this. "More than 800" is accurate for the 2012 reference.
- **d = 0.40 hinge point:** Confirmed. Hattie's d = 0.40 threshold as the boundary of "above average" instructional effect is accurately described and widely cited.
- **"d = 0.50 to d = 0.82" for cooperative discussion:** ⚠️ This range requires source verification. In Hattie's synthesis, "cooperative vs. competitive learning" shows d ≈ 0.54, and "cooperative vs. individualistic learning" shows d ≈ 0.59. Cooperative learning overall in the 2009 synthesis is reported at approximately d = 0.40–0.50 for the baseline, with variation by implementation quality. The upper bound of d = 0.82 is plausible for specific high-implementation studies but is not a commonly cited top-line Hattie figure for this category — it may reflect a specific sub-analysis. This precision warrants verification against the original Hattie tables before publication to avoid misrepresentation.

**Recommended action:** Confirm the d = 0.50–0.82 range against Hattie (2009 or 2012) appendices. If the range cannot be confirmed precisely, soften to: "effect sizes at or above d = 0.50 for high-quality implementations" — which is defensible without the upper bound.
