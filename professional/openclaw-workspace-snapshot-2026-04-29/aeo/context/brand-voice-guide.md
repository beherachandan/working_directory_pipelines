# Wayground Brand Voice Guide — Content Evaluation Reference
_Source: Wayground Comprehensive Style Guide (March 2026) — canonical brand voice reference._
_Imported: 2026-04-28_

## Voice Persona: The Tech Coach Aficionado
A supportive tech coach who's been in classroom trenches. Passionate about pedagogical excellence. Neither a distant academic nor a salesperson. The trusted colleague who makes teachers' lives easier.

## 5 Core Voice Principles
1. **Say it the teacher way** — "5 reading levels? That's a normal day at Wayground." (not "Equip every student with evidence-based differentiation.")
2. **Be a colleague, not a vendor** — "No more password resets or missing assignments." (not "Optimize learning with LMS, LTI, and rostering solutions.")
3. **Respect educators' time with clarity** — "Spend time teaching students, not hunting for resources." (not "Focus on what matters most when instruction is all in one place.")
4. **Stay clever, not cute** — "Students complete work when it feels like a win." (not "Gamify for growth.")
5. **Frame problems, focus on solutions** — Lead with the pain; end with the resolution.

## Tone Calibration
More: Confident, Knowledgeable, Supportive, Practical, Empathetic
Less: Boastful, Lecturing, Patronizing, Theoretical, Emotional

## HARD RULES (Violations = Immediate Brand FAIL)

### Naming
- ALWAYS: "Wayground"
- NEVER: "Quizizz" (unless: "formerly known as Quizizz" — first mention only, through end of 2026)
- NEVER: "quiz tool," "quiz app," "quiz maker," "quiz platform"
- ALWAYS: "supplemental learning platform" or "activity" as safe defaults

### Punctuation
- **Em dashes in blog copy: ZERO TOLERANCE** — not a single em dash (—) in any blog post
- Max 1 em dash per email
- Oxford commas: always present

### Headings
- **Sentence case ALWAYS** — capitalize only first word and proper nouns/product names
- NEVER title case headings (e.g., "How To Differentiate In The Classroom" is WRONG)
- NEVER open any heading with a question — headings state, they don't ask
  - **Exception (2026-04-28, confirmed by Chandan):** `/learn/` hub AEO articles MAY use question headings (H2/H3) when each question is immediately followed by a direct answer. This is an approved AEO pattern for knowledge-base articles. Does NOT apply to GTM/Sales/marketing copy where the original rule stands.

### Article Openings
- NEVER open with a question (first sentence of article)
- NEVER open with a product mention as the first sentence

### Superlatives & Claims
- NEVER: "revolutionary," "game-changing," "best-in-class" (without proof)
- NEVER: "just," "simply" (condescending minimizers)
- NEVER: "should," "must" (prescriptive tone)
- NEVER: "saves time" as a standalone claim — always follow with "time for what?"

### Positioning
- NEVER position Wayground as HQIM or core curriculum
- Always "supplemental" — supports Eureka, Bridges, IM, CKLA, Amplify, not replaces them

### AI Framing
- AI as supportive tool only
- NEVER: "AI replaces teachers," "AI does the work," overpromising AI capabilities
- ALWAYS: "AI-supported, teacher-first"

## Writing Standards

### Language
- Standard American English, 7th-grade reading level
- Present tense, first/second person ("you," "your students")
- Active voice
- One key idea per sentence

### Sentence & Paragraph
- 15–25 words per sentence; vary for rhythm
- 2–4 sentences per paragraph (max)
- Lead with the benefit

### Blog Post Format Standards
- Headline: 50–60 characters
- Intro: **60–80 words** (hard cap — shorter = better for AEO)
- Body: 800–1,200 words (How-to: 1,500–2,000; Topic-lead: 1,200–1,800; product-adjacent: 1,000–1,500)
- H2 opener paragraphs: **under 40 words** (AEO extraction — 2.7× more AI citations)
- Conclusion: 50–75 words
- Subheads: every 2–3 paragraphs

### Product Mentions
- Max **2–3 natural product mentions** per article
- Never open with a product mention
- Mentions must be value-adding, not promotional

### Owned Voice (AEO articles)
- Articles must read as Wayground's perspective, not as a literature review. Research supports the argument; it is not the argument.
- NEVER dedicate a whole section to summarising what researchers found. Say what you believe to be true, then back it with one supporting point if needed.
- NEVER open a paragraph with a researcher's name or a citation. Lead with the claim or the teaching implication. Bring in the source as backing, never as the headline.
- Bloom's Taxonomy levels, Piaget stages, and similar frameworks: name the level if it's genuinely useful context, but explain what it means for the classroom. "Based on Bloom's Level 4" is fine; a parenthetical `(Bloom's Level 4 — Analyze)` as a label is not.
- Every insight should feel like a knowledgeable colleague sharing what they've learned from experience, backed by evidence. Not a professor citing their bibliography.

### Citations & Evidence
- Named citations: "According to [Source, Year]" format
- Expert quotes: full attribution (name + title + organization)
- NEVER: "research shows" without a named source (generic = weak)
- NEVER fabricate statistics or quotes
- NEVER append raw parenthetical citation blocks after sentences — e.g., `(Abrami, P.C., Bernard, R.M., et al., *Review of Educational Research*, 2015)`. Attribution belongs woven into the sentence: "a 2015 study in the *Review of Educational Research*..."
- NEVER introduce statistical variables cold — g+, d=, p < values, effect sizes, percentages must be explained in plain English first. Say what the finding means to a teacher before (or instead of) giving the number. The number is supporting evidence, not the headline.
- NEVER use academic jargon without definition — "quasi-experimental and true experimental studies", "weighted mean effect size", etc. Either translate it or omit it. The reader is a classroom teacher, not a researcher.
- Use `—` (em dash) for parenthetical asides in article body copy, not `--` (double hyphen). Use `–` (en dash) for ranges (grades 5–12, 20–30 min).

## AI Content Rules
- Explain AI features in practical, classroom terms
- Show concrete classroom applications
- AI framed as supportive, human-centered
- Never present AI as teacher replacement or overpromise capabilities
