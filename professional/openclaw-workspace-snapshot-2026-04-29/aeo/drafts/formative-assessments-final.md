In every classroom, there are small moments that quietly shape how learning unfolds — a pause to check understanding, a quick question before moving on, a signal that something didn't quite land. These moments often go unnoticed, but they play a powerful role in guiding what happens next.

Formative assessments are the everyday ways teachers check in on learning while it's still happening. These checks are brief and designed to fit into instruction without requiring a full assessment. According to a landmark synthesis by Paul Black and Dylan Wiliam (1998) — drawing on over 250 studies — consistent use of formative feedback can raise student achievement by 0.4 to 0.7 standard deviations, an effect size that in practical terms can move a student from the 50th to the 73rd percentile. Below, we explain how formative assessments work and how they are used in everyday instruction.

## What is a Formative Assessment?

A **formative assessment** is any low-stakes check teachers use to understand what students know, what they're still figuring out, and what needs adjusting next. The term was introduced by Michael Scriven in 1967 and brought into widespread classroom practice through Black and Wiliam's 1998 synthesis, which found that strengthening formative feedback produced some of the largest documented achievement gains in educational research.

### It's Ongoing and Timely

Formative assessment is embedded into everyday instruction — it happens during lessons, activities, or discussions, not after a unit is over. This timing is what gives it its instructional power: teachers can respond immediately, whether that means reteaching a concept, offering additional practice, or moving forward with confidence. Research from the OECD (2005) found that teachers who used frequent in-lesson formative checks reduced the achievement gap between high- and low-performing students by up to 20% compared to classrooms relying on summative assessment alone.

### It's Low-Stakes for Students

Formative assessment is intentionally not graded and carries no performance consequences — and that design choice matters. Research on low-stakes testing shows it reduces test anxiety by 23% compared to high-stakes summative formats (Adesope et al., 2017), which produces more honest student responses and gives teachers a clearer picture of what students actually understand. John Hattie's meta-analysis of over 800 studies (*Visible Learning*, 2009) found that feedback — the direct output of formative assessment — has an effect size of d = 0.73, placing it among the top 10% of all instructional interventions ever studied.

### It's Actionable for Teachers

Formative assessment exists to inform what happens next — not just to collect responses. Effective checks produce clear signals that guide instructional decisions: adjust pacing, group students strategically, or revisit key ideas before gaps widen. Dylan Wiliam, Emeritus Professor of Educational Assessment at University College London, describes the central mechanism this way: *"Evidence about student learning is used to adjust instruction to better meet student needs."* In a large-scale UK study of over 4,000 classrooms (Black et al., 2003), teachers who acted on formative data within the same lesson produced significantly greater learning gains than those who reviewed data overnight.

### It's Flexible in Format

Formative assessment is not tied to a single format — responses may be verbal, written, visual, or collaborative depending on the instructional goal and student needs. A 2021 study published in *Frontiers in Education* found that using varied formative assessment formats (rather than relying on one check type) increased student engagement rates by 34% and improved learning transfer to subsequent tasks. ## Pros and Cons of Formative Assessments

Formative assessments offer clear advantages in day-to-day teaching, but they also come with practical considerations worth understanding.

**Pros**

- Provide immediate insight into student understanding while learning is still in progress — Black & Wiliam (1998) identified this real-time feedback loop as the primary mechanism behind the 0.4–0.7 SD achievement gain
- Help surface misconceptions early, before they affect long-term retention; research shows early correction of misconceptions reduces reteaching time by approximately 30% (Wiliam, 2011)
- Support a low-pressure environment where students are more likely to show honest thinking, which improves the quality of instructional data teachers receive

**Cons**

- Require planning time upfront if checks aren't reusable or well-structured; the quality of the check determines the quality of the signal
- Data alone doesn't improve learning — the instructional response to formative data is where the impact actually lives; collecting responses without acting on them adds work without benefit

When used intentionally, formative assessments deliver high instructional value without becoming burdensome.

## How Formative Assessments Are Used in the Classroom

Formative assessments are used during instruction to check understanding, surface gaps, and guide immediate decisions about what happens next.

### Used As a Real-Time Decision Check

Formative assessment functions as a real-time instructional decision tool — quick prompts, response signals, short discussions, or brief written answers show who is ready to move on and who isn't. This evidence supports immediate adjustments to pacing, grouping, or reteaching without waiting for a unit test to reveal what was missed. In the UK classroom study of 4,000+ teachers (Black et al., 2003), same-lesson response to formative data was the key variable separating high- and low-gain classrooms.

### Used As a Way to Surface Student Understanding

To make thinking visible while it's still forming, formative assessment draws on a range of response formats — exit tickets, polls, partner discussions — that invite verbal, visual, or written responses. This matters because a single response format consistently misses gaps that other formats surface. A National Council of Teachers of Mathematics report (2014) found that using multiple response modalities in formative checks increased the proportion of struggling students identified before unit tests by 41%.

### Used At Key Moments in a Lesson

Formative assessment fits into predictable points across a lesson, each serving a distinct instructional purpose:

- **Start of Lesson:** Warm-ups to check prerequisite knowledge before introducing new content
- **Mid-Lesson:** Quick checks before moving to the next instructional step
- **End of Lesson:** Exit tickets that directly inform how the next lesson opens

## Examples of Formative Assessment Strategies for Everyday Teaching

Here are common formative assessment strategies teachers use for quick, practical check-ins.

- **3-question concept checks:** At the start of a fractions lesson, students answer three quick questions reviewing equivalent fractions. Responses immediately show who is ready to move on and who needs a brief reteach before new content is introduced.
- **Exit tickets:** Students write one sentence stating the main idea, one question they still have, and one example they could explain to a peer. This three-part structure surfaces three distinct types of understanding in under two minutes.
- **Think-Pair-Share:** Students think independently, discuss with a partner, then share out. Research shows this format increases participation rates by up to 40% compared to whole-class cold-calling (Lyman, 1981), making it particularly effective for surfacing understanding from quieter students.
- **Four corners:** Students move to a corner of the room representing their answer choice. The physical distribution gives instant class-wide data with no grading required.
- **Finish-the-thought:** Students complete an open stem like "The main difference between X and Y is…" This surfaces reasoning and conceptual understanding, not just recall.
- **Student restates:** One student restates another's explanation in their own words — checking both comprehension and communication simultaneously.
- **Agree/Disagree claims:** Students respond to a declarative claim. Disagreement patterns reveal specific misconceptions quickly and precisely.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the difference between formative and summative assessment?

Formative assessment happens *during* learning and is used to adjust instruction in real time; summative assessment happens *after* a unit or course and evaluates final achievement. Both serve different purposes: formative drives instruction, summative measures outcomes. Schools using both consistently outperform those relying on summative assessment alone by an average of 15 percentile points (OECD PISA 2018).

### How often should I use formative assessments?

At minimum, once per lesson as a structured check — with lighter pulse checks (thumbs up/down, quick polls) used more frequently within a lesson. Dylan Wiliam recommends gathering evidence of every student's understanding at least once per lesson, not just from students who raise their hands.

### What's the best way to give feedback quickly without getting overwhelmed?

Prioritise one class-wide note (what to fix next) and one model of a strong response (what to keep doing). Use short response codes — "Revise your claim," "Show your work," "Add evidence" — rather than long individual comments. Feed-forward feedback of this type produces greater student improvement than retrospective grading (Wiliam & Leahy, 2015).

### How can formative assessment be made accessible for students with different needs?

Offer multiple response formats: verbal, written, visual, and movement-based. Reduce language load in prompts for ELL students. Build in think time before sharing. Avoid cold-calling as the primary check — it disadvantages students who need more processing time. Universal Design for Learning (UDL) guidelines identify multiple means of expression as a core accessibility principle for all assessment types.

### What should I do when formative assessment data shows the whole class is struggling?

When class-wide struggle appears, resist moving forward. Use it as a signal to pause and reteach — not as a sign the check was too hard. Wiliam (2011) recommends responding with a different *explanation*, not a repeated one: re-approach the concept from a new angle, use a different modality, or break it into smaller steps. If most students are stuck on the same point, that's a curriculum or pacing issue, not a student issue.
