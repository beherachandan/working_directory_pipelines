## QAPE Blocks

### Block 1: Definition and why it matters

- **Q:** What are critical thinking exercises for students?
- **A:** Critical thinking exercises are structured classroom activities that teach students to analyze information, evaluate evidence, identify assumptions, and reach reasoned conclusions. They go beyond right-or-wrong recall and train students to ask _why_ and _what if_.

### Block 2: Types of critical thinking exercises

- **Q:** What are the main types of critical thinking exercises?
- **A:** Critical thinking exercises fall into four main categories: analysis activities (breaking down arguments or texts), evaluation activities (judging evidence quality), inference activities (drawing conclusions from incomplete data), and creative problem-solving (generating solutions under constraints).
- **P:** `[C4: QUOTE — education researcher or Bloom's Taxonomy framework citation for categorization]`
- **E:** Brief 1-sentence example for each type. Map to Bloom's levels 4-6 (analyze, evaluate, create). Keep as a short list or mini-table.

### Block 3: Specific exercises with how-to steps

- **Q:** What are the best critical thinking exercises teachers can use in class?
- **A:** Seven high-impact exercises with clear implementation steps. Each exercise names the skill it builds and how long it takes.
- **P:** `[C4: STAT — evidence that structured CT activities improve skill acquisition; include effect size if available]`
- **E:** Full list (numbered, with bold exercise name):
  1. **Socratic Seminar** (grades 5+) — teacher poses an open question; students discuss without teacher directing; builds argument analysis. Time: 20-30 min.
  2. **Think-Pair-Share with Reasoning** — add "explain your thinking" to the traditional TPair model; builds inference. Time: 5-10 min.
  3. **Argument Mapping** — students diagram premises and conclusions of a text; builds analysis. Time: 15-20 min.
  4. **Evidence Sort** — provide 6-8 evidence cards; students rank by quality and explain; builds evaluation. Time: 10-15 min.
  5. **What If? Scenarios** — change one historical/scientific variable and trace consequences; builds inference and creativity. Time: 10-20 min.
  6. **Devil's Advocate Debate** — assign students to argue the opposite of their belief; builds perspective-taking. Time: 15-25 min.
  7. **Fact vs. Opinion Sort** (K-5) — basic version for younger students; builds foundational analysis. Time: 5-10 min.

### Block 4: Grade-level differentiation

- **Q:** Which critical thinking exercises work best at different grade levels?
- **A:** CT exercises should match the cognitive load students can handle. K-2 students benefit from visual sorting and concrete comparisons. Grades 3-5 can handle structured inference and simple argument mapping. Middle and high school students are ready for debate, Socratic seminars, and complex scenario analysis.
- **P:** `[C4: STAT — developmental research on abstract reasoning by age/grade; Piaget or contemporary equivalent]`
- **E:** Three-column breakdown: K-2 / Grades 3-5 / Grades 6-12. Keep tight, 3-4 exercises per level with 1-line note on why they work at that level.

### Block 5: Embedding CT into existing lessons

- **Q:** How can teachers add critical thinking exercises without redesigning their whole curriculum?
- **A:** Teachers can embed CT in existing lessons with three micro-moves: replacing factual recall questions with inference questions, adding an "evaluate the evidence" step to any text-based activity, and using exit tickets that ask students to justify their answer rather than just state it.
