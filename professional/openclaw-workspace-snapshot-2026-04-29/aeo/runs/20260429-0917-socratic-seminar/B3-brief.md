## QAPE Blocks

### Block 1: What is a Socratic seminar?
- **Q:** What is a Socratic seminar?
- **A:** Define in first 2 sentences. Named after Socrates. A structured discussion where students explore a text or idea together through open-ended questioning rather than receiving teacher-delivered answers.
- **P:** [STAT: research on discussion-based learning outcomes] [QUOTE: education researcher on Socratic method]
- **E:** Origin in Socratic method. Key distinguishing feature: teacher as facilitator, not lecturer. Students build on each other's ideas. Epistemological premise — understanding deepens through dialogue.

### Block 2: How to run a Socratic seminar (step-by-step)
- **Q:** How do you run a Socratic seminar?
- **A:** 5-step process beginning with text selection and ending with debrief. State it clearly in first 2 sentences.
- **P:** [QUOTE: experienced educator on preparation being key to seminar quality]
- **E:** Step 1: Select an appropriate text (rich, ambiguous, open-ended). Step 2: Assign pre-reading with guiding focus questions. Step 3: Arrange seating (inner/outer circle or full circle). Step 4: Facilitate discussion with minimal intervention. Step 5: Structured debrief — what did we learn? What questions remain?

### Block 3: What formats do Socratic seminars take?
- **Q:** What are the different Socratic seminar formats?
- **A:** Three main formats. Name them in first sentence.
- **P:** [STAT or source on fishbowl discussion research]
- **E:** (1) Inner/outer circle (fishbowl): inner group discusses, outer observes and takes notes — then groups swap. Best for large classes. (2) Whole-class seminar: everyone participates simultaneously — good for smaller groups or experienced students. (3) Small group seminars: class divided into groups of 5-8 running simultaneous discussions — more airtime per student but harder to facilitate.

### Block 4: What makes a good Socratic seminar question?
- **Q:** What makes a good Socratic seminar question?
- **A:** Good questions are open-ended, text-based, and have no single right answer. State in first sentence.
- **P:** [QUOTE or research source on quality of discussion questions]
- **E:** Three types: (1) Opening questions that launch discussion (2) Core questions that deepen it (3) Closing questions that consolidate learning. Give 3-5 example questions across types. The best questions create genuine disagreement or uncertainty — if everyone agrees immediately, the question isn't rich enough.

### Block 5: Why are Socratic seminars effective?
- **Q:** Why are Socratic seminars effective?
- **A:** Research shows discussion-based learning improves comprehension, critical thinking, and retention compared to passive instruction. State in first 2 sentences.
- **P:** [STAT: meta-analysis on discussion-based learning outcomes] [STAT: critical thinking development research]
- **E:** Mechanisms: (1) Active retrieval — students must recall and apply knowledge (2) Perspective-taking — hearing other views expands understanding (3) Social accountability — students prepare more carefully when they know they'll discuss aloud (4) Language development — particularly for ELL students. Cite Vygotsky's zone of proximal development or Bloom's higher-order thinking alignment.

### Block 6: What does the teacher do during a Socratic seminar?
- **Q:** What is the teacher's role in a Socratic seminar?
- **A:** The teacher facilitates rather than teaches. Their role is to ask clarifying questions, maintain productive norms, and track participation — not to provide answers.
- **P:** [QUOTE: teacher or researcher on facilitation vs. instruction]
- **E:** Specific facilitation moves: redirecting questions back to the text, prompting quieter students without cold-calling, using "say more" and "what do others think?", taking observation notes for assessment.

### Block 7: Classroom examples by grade
- **Q:** What does a Socratic seminar look like at different grade levels?
- **A:** Socratic seminars can work from grades 3 through 12 with format and text adjustments. State in first sentence.
- **P:** [STAT or source on discussion in elementary school research]
- **E:** Elementary (3-5): shorter texts (picture books, short articles), tighter facilitation, guided questions provided. Middle (6-8): literary texts, current events articles, structured roles. High school (9-12): primary sources, complex texts, student-generated questions.
