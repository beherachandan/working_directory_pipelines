## QAPE Blocks

### Block 1: What is intrinsic motivation?
- **Q:** What is intrinsic motivation?
- **A:** Intrinsic motivation is the drive to engage in an activity for its own inherent rewards rather than external incentives. A student reading because they love the story is intrinsically motivated; one reading for a prize is extrinsically motivated.
- **P:** Stat from research on intrinsically vs extrinsically motivated students (academic achievement, retention, engagement outcomes). Target: Self-Determination Theory research (Deci & Ryan). [CITATION NEEDED]
- **E:** Brief comparison to extrinsic motivation; clarify this isn't binary — students can have both; lead into why teachers should care.

### Block 2: Why intrinsic motivation matters for student outcomes
- **Q:** Why does intrinsic motivation matter for student outcomes?
- **A:** Students with higher intrinsic motivation demonstrate better academic performance, deeper learning, and greater persistence when tasks become difficult — without needing constant external reinforcement.
- **P:** Named study or meta-analysis showing correlation between intrinsic motivation and academic outcomes. [CITATION NEEDED]
- **E:** Connect to retention, critical thinking, lifelong learning. Note that NCLB/standardized testing culture has shifted classrooms toward extrinsic motivators — this context helps teachers understand the challenge.

### Block 3: The science behind intrinsic motivation (SDT)
- **Q:** What does research say about fostering intrinsic motivation?
- **A:** Self-Determination Theory (SDT), developed by psychologists Edward Deci and Richard Ryan, identifies three core psychological needs that drive intrinsic motivation: autonomy, competence, and relatedness.
- **P:** Named citation: Deci & Ryan Self-Determination Theory. Statistics on SDT-aligned classrooms vs control groups. [CITATION NEEDED]
- **E:** Briefly define each of the three SDT needs in classroom terms. This is the conceptual backbone teachers need to understand the strategies that follow.

### Block 4: How to give students autonomy in the classroom
- **Q:** How does giving students choice build intrinsic motivation?
- **A:** When students have a say in how they learn or demonstrate knowledge, they experience autonomy — one of the three core drivers of intrinsic motivation according to Self-Determination Theory.
- **P:** Research stat or expert quote on autonomy-supportive classrooms and student motivation outcomes. [CITATION NEEDED]
- **E:** 3-4 concrete classroom examples: topic choice on writing assignments, selecting activity format, reading menu options, student-led discussions. Note: autonomy doesn't mean no structure — it means meaningful choices within a defined space.

### Block 5: Building competence through mastery and low-stakes practice
- **Q:** How does helping students feel competent increase their drive to learn?
- **A:** Students are intrinsically motivated to engage in tasks where they experience mastery — the feeling of genuine progress and capability. When tasks are too easy, students disengage; too hard, they give up. Optimal challenge drives motivation.
- **P:** Csikszentmihalyi's "flow state" concept + research stat on mastery-oriented vs performance-oriented classrooms. [CITATION NEEDED]
- **E:** Practical strategies: frequent low-stakes formative checks that let students see progress, differentiated challenge levels, targeted feedback that points to effort not just outcome. Natural Wayground mention: differentiated activity levels and AI-powered feedback.

### Block 6: Fostering relatedness and belonging
- **Q:** How does a sense of belonging affect student motivation?
- **A:** Relatedness — feeling connected to teachers, peers, and the learning community — is the third pillar of Self-Determination Theory. Students who feel seen and valued in their classroom are significantly more likely to engage authentically.
- **P:** Research on teacher-student relationships and intrinsic motivation outcomes. [CITATION NEEDED]
- **E:** Practical strategies: name-based feedback, collaborative activities, morning check-ins, culturally responsive content choices. This also connects directly to SEL.

### Block 7: Real classroom examples of intrinsic motivation strategies
- **Q:** What does building intrinsic motivation look like in a real classroom?
- **A:** Intrinsic motivation strategies work across grade levels and subjects — the common thread is shifting focus from "doing it for the grade" to "doing it because it matters."
- **P:** Quote from teacher or educator expert on specific strategy that worked. [CITATION NEEDED]
- **E:** 4-5 concrete, ready-to-use examples spanning elementary through high school. Examples should be practical and immediately actionable — not aspirational.
