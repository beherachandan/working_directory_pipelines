# What is response to intervention (RTI)?

Schools have faced the same problem for decades: students fall behind, and no one catches it until the gap is already wide. Response to Intervention was designed to change that.

Response to Intervention, or RTI, is a multi-tiered, data-driven school framework that identifies struggling students early and delivers targeted support before they fall significantly behind. It uses a three-tier system of increasing instructional intensity, with ongoing progress monitoring to determine whether students are responding to the support they receive. This guide explains how each tier works, what the classroom teacher's role is, and how RTI connects to special education identification.

---

## What is response to intervention (RTI)

Response to Intervention is a structured school framework that emerged from the reauthorization of the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) in 2004. Before RTI, many schools used an "ability-achievement discrepancy" model to identify learning disabilities: students had to fail severely before receiving a diagnosis. RTI replaced that wait-to-fail approach with proactive, tiered support.

Under IDEA 2004, Section 614(b)(6), schools are explicitly permitted to use a student's response to scientific, research-based intervention as part of the determination of specific learning disability (SLD) eligibility. This legal foundation is what distinguishes RTI from previous intervention frameworks and makes it a recognized pathway in the special education identification process.

The framework has two core components: a tiered instructional system that matches support intensity to student need, and a data system that monitors whether students are responding and guides decisions about when to change the intensity of support.

---

## The three tiers of RTI explained

RTI uses a pyramid model with three levels of support. The base of the pyramid is Tier 1, which serves all students. As students demonstrate they need more support, they move up the pyramid to Tier 2 and then Tier 3.

### Tier 1: Core instruction for all students

Tier 1 is high-quality, evidence-based instruction delivered by the general education teacher to the entire class. The benchmark: approximately 80% of students should meet grade-level expectations through Tier 1 instruction alone.

According to the RTI Action Network and National Research Center on Learning Disabilities guidelines, the population benchmarks are approximately 80% at Tier 1, 15-20% at Tier 2, and 5% at Tier 3. These are benchmarks for instructional planning, not rigid cutoffs. When fewer than 80% of students meet benchmarks, the problem is instructional, not individual. Before adding students to Tier 2, the team should examine whether core instruction is actually evidence-based and delivered with fidelity. (Fuchs & Fuchs, 2006; National Research Center on Learning Disabilities, 2006)

### Tier 2: Targeted small-group intervention

Tier 2 adds targeted, standardized intervention for the approximately 15-20% of students not meeting benchmarks through Tier 1 instruction alone. Groups are small, typically three to five students, and sessions run 20-30 minutes three to five times per week for 8-12 weeks.

Interventions at this tier use research-validated programs and are delivered in addition to, not instead of, regular Tier 1 instruction. Progress is monitored every one to two weeks to track whether students are responding.

### Tier 3: Intensive, individualized support

Tier 3 serves the approximately 5% of students with the most significant needs. Instruction is more intensive: sessions of 45-60 minutes daily or near-daily, in groups of one to three students or individual sessions. Progress monitoring increases to weekly.

Students who do not show adequate progress at Tier 3 after a full intervention cycle are typically referred for a comprehensive special education evaluation. As Vaughn and Fuchs (2003) established, RTI's origins as a learning disability identification model rest on the principle that inadequate response to increasingly intensive, research-based intervention is itself meaningful diagnostic evidence.

## Universal screening: the foundation of RTI

Universal screening means testing all students, not just those who have been referred or flagged, at regular intervals to identify who needs additional support before they fall behind. Screening is brief, standardized, and repeated three times per year: typically fall, winter, and spring.

Early identification through universal screening is significantly more accurate than waiting for teacher referral. Research by Compton, Fuchs, Fuchs, and Bryant (2006) found that combining one-time screening with five weeks of weekly progress monitoring nearly eliminated false positives in early reading identification at first grade, a finding that demonstrates the value of screening data over subjective referral.

Common screening tools include:
- **DIBELS Next** (Dynamic Indicators of Basic Early Literacy Skills) — reading fluency and phonics, grades K-8
- **AIMSweb Plus** — reading and math, grades K-12
- **FastBridge** — reading, math, and behavior, grades K-12
- **easyCBM** — reading and math, grades K-8

Screening identifies who may need support. It does not diagnose. Students who fall below benchmark on a screener receive additional diagnostic assessment before intervention programs are selected.

## How progress monitoring works in RTI

Progress monitoring is the ongoing data collection that tells teachers and RTI teams whether an intervention is working. It uses brief, standardized assessments called curriculum-based measures (CBMs) that can be administered in one to three minutes and repeated frequently without testing fatigue.

Stecker, Fuchs, and Fuchs (2005) reviewed the research on CBM-based progress monitoring and found that teachers who used CBM data to adjust instruction produced significantly stronger student learning outcomes compared to teachers who relied on subjective judgment alone. The key variable was not just collecting data, but using it to make instructional decisions.

Data from progress monitoring is graphed over time as a trend line. Teams compare the trend line to a goal line, the rate of improvement a student needs to reach grade-level benchmarks by the end of the intervention period. Decision rules are applied based on patterns in the data, typically after 6-8 data points, not individual scores.

Progress monitoring frequency by tier:
- Tier 2: every 1-2 weeks
- Tier 3: weekly

## How RTI teams use data to make decisions

RTI is not a referral pipeline. It is a decision-making system. The question at each data review is not "Does this student have a learning disability?" but "Is this student responding to the current level of support, and what does the data say about what comes next?"

"The purpose of progress monitoring in RTI is not to generate paperwork but to drive instructional change," notes Lynn Fuchs, Dunn Family Professor of Psychoeducational Assessment at Vanderbilt University, who co-developed many of the foundational CBM measures used in RTI progress monitoring today. "Data only serves students when teams are prepared to act on what it shows."

RTI teams typically include the general education teacher, a reading or math specialist, a school psychologist, and an administrator. They meet regularly, review progress monitoring graphs, and ask three questions:
1. Is the student's trend line above or below the goal line?
2. Has the student had adequate exposure to the intervention, with fidelity?
3. What does the pattern say about the next step?

If progress is adequate, the student remains at the current tier. If progress is inadequate after a full intervention period, the team intensifies support and, if necessary at Tier 3, initiates a special education evaluation referral.

## RTI vs MTSS: what is the difference

RTI and MTSS are closely related frameworks that many schools and districts use interchangeably, but they are not identical.

RTI originated as a framework specifically for academic intervention and learning disability identification. MTSS, or Multi-Tiered System of Supports, is a broader framework that uses the same tiered structure but extends it to include behavior support, social-emotional learning, attendance, and other student wellness systems alongside academic intervention.

According to the National Technical Assistance Center on Positive Behavioral Interventions and Supports (PBIS.org), MTSS combines RTI for academics with PBIS for behavior within a unified, school-wide tiered framework. IDEA 2004 specifically references RTI for learning disability identification; MTSS has no equivalent federal mandate but has been adopted as the umbrella framework at the state level across most U.S. states.

A useful way to think about the relationship: RTI sits inside MTSS. PBIS addresses behavior. RTI addresses academics. SEL support addresses social-emotional development. Together, they form an MTSS system.

## The classroom teacher's role in RTI

The classroom teacher is the most important person in the RTI system, because Tier 1 instruction is where the framework starts, and Tier 1 quality determines how many students need more intensive support.

A review and synthesis of the RTI literature found that general education teachers' Tier 1 responsibilities include screening, selecting research-based instructional strategies, differentiating within the classroom, and monitoring students' progress to inform decision-making. (Fuchs, Fuchs, & Speece, 2002; Kovaleski & Pedersen, 2008)

The classroom teacher's specific Tier 1 responsibilities include:
- Delivering evidence-based instruction using strategies validated by research
- Participating in universal screening and reviewing screening data
- Differentiating instruction within the classroom for students who need it
- Referring students who are not responding to classroom differentiation for Tier 2 consideration
- Collecting and sharing classroom performance data with the RTI team

Teachers can use formative assessment activities to track which students are not mastering grade-level content before the gap widens. ## How RTI connects to special education

IDEA 2004 explicitly permits schools to use a student's response to scientific, research-based intervention as part of the process for determining whether a student has a specific learning disability (SLD). RTI data does not replace a full evaluation, but it provides documented evidence that targeted instruction was provided and that the student did or did not respond.

The legal basis is IDEA 2004, Section 614(b)(6), which states that schools may not use a discrepancy between achievement and intellectual ability as the sole determinant of SLD and may instead use data from a process that determines response to scientific, research-based intervention.

Key clarifications:
- RTI participation does not prevent or delay a request for a special education evaluation. Parents can request an evaluation at any time, regardless of the student's tier status.
- RTI data becomes part of the student's evaluation record and can inform eligibility decisions, IEP development, and service planning.
- A student can receive both RTI services and a special education evaluation simultaneously.

## Common RTI implementation mistakes

RTI works when implemented well. Research shows that when RTI outcomes disappoint, the cause is almost always implementation quality, not the framework itself.

The most significant large-scale evaluation of RTI implementation, conducted by Balu, Zhu, Doolittle, Schiller, Jenkins, and Gersten for the National Center for Education Evaluation (NCEE 2016-4000), examined RTI practices across 146 schools and found highly variable outcomes tied directly to implementation quality. Schools with strong Tier 1 core instruction showed markedly different outcomes from schools that had weak universal instruction but high Tier 2/3 referral rates.

The five most common implementation failures:

1. **Poor Tier 1 quality.** When fewer than 80% of students meet grade-level benchmarks, the problem is often the core instruction, not the individual students. Skipping Tier 1 improvement and jumping to Tier 2 referrals does not fix the underlying problem.

2. **Interventions chosen by availability, not evidence.** Not every program marketed as an "RTI intervention" has strong research support. Interventions should be selected from validated, peer-reviewed sources such as the What Works Clearinghouse or the Florida Center for Reading Research that show evidence of effectiveness at the targeted skill level.

3. **Progress monitoring data collected but not used.** Data that does not inform decisions provides no benefit. Teams need structured protocols for reviewing data and clear decision rules for when to change a student's intervention.

4. **Fidelity not checked.** An evidence-based intervention delivered inconsistently is not evidence-based in practice. Schools need a system for verifying that interventions are being delivered as designed.

5. **Treating RTI as a special education referral pipeline.** When RTI becomes "three tiers before special ed," it distorts the framework and delays appropriate support for students who need evaluation sooner.

## Frequently asked questions about RTI

**What does RTI stand for?**
RTI stands for Response to Intervention. It is also sometimes called Response to Instruction and Intervention (RTII) or, more broadly, Multi-Tiered System of Supports (MTSS) when behavior and SEL support are included alongside academic intervention.

**What is the difference between RTI and an IEP?**
RTI is a general education framework that provides tiered support to all students, regardless of disability status. An IEP (Individualized Education Program) is a legal document required for students who qualify for special education services under IDEA. RTI data may inform an IEP, and students with IEPs may also receive RTI support, but they are separate systems with different legal purposes.

**What is the difference between RTI and MTSS?**
RTI is primarily an academic intervention framework with roots in special education law. MTSS is a broader framework that applies the same tiered logic to academics, behavior, and social-emotional support simultaneously. Many schools use MTSS as the umbrella system with RTI as one component.

**How long should a student be in Tier 2 before moving to Tier 3?**
The standard Tier 2 duration is 8-12 weeks of consistent intervention before the team evaluates progress and makes a tier decision. Teams use 6-8 progress monitoring data points, not just the final score, to determine whether a student's trajectory is adequate. If the trend line is consistently below the goal line after a full intervention cycle, the team considers Tier 3.

**Can a student be evaluated for special education while receiving RTI services?**
Yes. A parent can request a formal special education evaluation at any time, regardless of the student's current tier status. RTI participation does not create a prerequisite period before evaluation can occur. The school must either conduct the evaluation or provide written notice with the reasons for refusal within specific timelines under IDEA.

---

## The core idea in RTI

RTI shifts schools from reactive to proactive. Instead of waiting for students to fail before providing support, it catches struggling learners early and delivers targeted help at the level of intensity they need. The framework works when Tier 1 instruction is strong, interventions are evidence-based, data drives decisions, and the classroom teacher understands their central role. Tier 1 is where everything begins.
