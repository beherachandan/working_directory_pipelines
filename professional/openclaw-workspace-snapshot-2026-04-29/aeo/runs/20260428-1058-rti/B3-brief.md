## QAPE Blocks

### Block 1: RTI definition
- **Q:** What is response to intervention (RTI)?
- **A:** Response to Intervention, or RTI, is a multi-tiered school framework that identifies struggling students early and delivers targeted interventions before they fall significantly behind. It replaces the "wait-to-fail" model with proactive, data-driven support at increasing levels of intensity.
- **P:** STAT NEEDED: IDEA 2004 legislative foundation reference, OR National RTI implementation statistic (how many schools/districts use RTI), OR early identification impact stat. C4 to inject.
- **E:** Brief history: RTI emerged from IDEA 2004 reauthorization as an alternative to ability-achievement discrepancy model for identifying learning disabilities. Now widely adopted; often part of broader MTSS framework.

### Block 2: Three tiers
- **Q:** What are the three tiers of RTI?
- **A:** RTI uses a three-tier pyramid model. Tier 1 is high-quality instruction for all students; Tier 2 adds targeted small-group intervention for students not meeting benchmarks; Tier 3 provides intensive, individualized support for students with the most significant needs.
- **P:** STAT REQUIRED (already in prior article — keep exact): 80% of students served in Tier 1; 15-20% in Tier 2; 5% in Tier 3. Source: standard RTI population distribution framework. ALSO add: STAT NEEDED on Tier 1 instructional quality impact — C4 to find specific citation (e.g., Vaughn & Fuchs 2003 or Burns & Gibbons).
- **E:** 
  - Tier 1: Universal instruction delivered to all students by general ed teacher. High-quality, evidence-based. 80% goal = if fewer than 80% meet benchmark, Tier 1 needs improvement, not just individual students.
  - Tier 2: Small group (3-5 students), 20-30 min / 3-5x per week for 8-12 weeks. Targeted, standardized intervention program. Progress monitored every 1-2 weeks.
  - Tier 3: Intensive, individualized. 45-60 min daily or near-daily. Small group (1-3 students) or 1:1. Progress monitored weekly. May precede or run concurrently with special ed evaluation.

### Block 3: Universal screening
- **Q:** What is universal screening in RTI?
- **A:** Universal screening means assessing all students, not just those flagged as struggling, at regular intervals to identify who needs additional support before they fall behind. Screening is brief, standardized, and administered three times per year.
- **P:** STAT NEEDED: Research finding on early identification effectiveness via universal screening vs. teacher referral alone. C4 to inject (Glover & DiPerna, 2007 or similar).
- **E:** Common tools: DIBELS Next (reading), AIMSweb Plus (reading and math), easyCBM, FastBridge. What a screener tests vs. what a full diagnostic assessment tests (distinction). Timeline: fall, winter, spring.

### Block 4: Progress monitoring
- **Q:** How does progress monitoring work in RTI?
- **A:** Progress monitoring uses brief, repeated assessments — called curriculum-based measures (CBMs) — to track whether a student's response to an intervention is on track. Data is graphed over time, and decision rules determine when to adjust or intensify the intervention.
- **P:** STAT NEEDED: Research on CBM validity and reliability for RTI decision-making, OR stat on intervention adjustment outcomes when progress monitoring data is used consistently. C4 to inject (Deno, 2003 or Stecker, Fuchs & Fuchs, 2005).
- **E:** Frequency: Tier 2 = biweekly; Tier 3 = weekly. Goal line vs. trend line analysis. What triggers a tier change (not a number, but pattern over 6-8 data points). Aimsweb and DIBELS both have built-in progress monitoring norms.

### Block 5: Data-based decision making
- **Q:** How do teachers use RTI data to make instructional decisions?
- **A:** RTI requires teachers to act on student data at regular intervals rather than waiting for report cards or end-of-year assessments. Decision-making follows structured protocols: if a student's trend line consistently falls below the goal line, the team increases support.
- **P:** STAT or QUOTE NEEDED: Expert citation on data-based decision making in RTI — how teams use 6-8 data points before a tier change, fidelity requirements. C4 to inject.
- **E:** The RTI team process: who is involved (general ed teacher, reading specialist, school psychologist, administrator), how often they meet, what data they review. Decision tree: inadequate progress at Tier 2 for full intervention duration → move to Tier 3. Inadequate at Tier 3 → trigger special ed evaluation.

### Block 6: RTI vs MTSS
- **Q:** What is the difference between RTI and MTSS?
- **A:** RTI and MTSS are related but not identical. RTI is the academic intervention component; MTSS (Multi-Tiered System of Supports) is a broader framework that encompasses RTI but also includes behavior support, SEL, and other student wellness systems within the same tiered structure.
- **P:** STAT or EXPERT QUOTE NEEDED: Citation clarifying RTI as academic/LD-identification focus vs. MTSS as whole-child framework. Many districts and states use the terms interchangeably; this distinction is worth naming. C4 to inject.
- **E:** Venn diagram explanation in text: RTI sits inside MTSS. PBIS (behavior) + RTI (academics) + SEL support = MTSS. Note: IDEA 2004 specifically references RTI for LD identification; MTSS has no equivalent federal mandate but is widely adopted at state level.

### Block 7: Teacher's role in Tier 1
- **Q:** What is the classroom teacher's role in RTI?
- **A:** The classroom teacher owns Tier 1 — the universal, high-quality instruction delivered to all students. Strong Tier 1 reduces the number of students who need more intensive support. When Tier 1 is weak, the problem is instructional, not individual.
- **P:** STAT NEEDED: Research finding on Tier 1 quality and referral rates — e.g., when high-quality core instruction is in place, Tier 2 referral rates drop significantly. C4 to inject.
- **E:** Practical Tier 1 responsibilities: use evidence-based instructional strategies, screen all students, respond to screening data by differentiating within the classroom, refer students not responding to differentiation for Tier 2. Wayground angle: use formative assessment activities to identify students who are not mastering grade-level content before they fall behind — natural, non-promotional mention.

### Block 8: RTI and special education
- **Q:** How does RTI connect to special education referral?
- **A:** IDEA 2004 permits schools to use a student's response to research-based intervention as part of the process for determining specific learning disability (SLD) eligibility. RTI data does not replace a full evaluation but can serve as documented evidence that targeted instruction was attempted before referral.
- **P:** STAT: IDEA 2004 Section 614 reference — legal basis for using RTI data in LD identification. This is factual legal language, not a percentage stat — C4 to verify and format properly.
- **E:** Key clarification: RTI participation does not prevent or delay a special education evaluation request. Parents can request evaluation at any point. RTI data becomes part of the student's evaluation record. Note: this section should be factual, not prescriptive.

### Block 9: Common implementation mistakes
- **Q:** What are the most common RTI implementation mistakes?
- **A:** The three most common RTI implementation failures are: poor Tier 1 quality (the 80% benchmark isn't met, but the school focuses on identifying individuals rather than fixing instruction), lack of intervention fidelity, and infrequent or inconsistent progress monitoring.
- **P:** STAT or QUOTE NEEDED: Expert citation on RTI implementation failures — Balu et al., 2015 (NCEE report on RTI effectiveness) is the landmark study and showed implementation fidelity was the key variable. C4 to inject.
- **E:** 5 common mistakes:
  1. Treating RTI as a special education referral pathway rather than a tiered support system
  2. Tier 1 instruction is not evidence-based — RTI is only as strong as its foundation
  3. Intervention programs chosen by availability, not evidence base
  4. Progress monitoring data collected but not used for decisions
  5. Missing fidelity checks — are teachers delivering the intervention as designed?

### Block 10: Wayground and RTI
- **Q:** How can classroom technology support RTI implementation?
- **A:** Technology that reduces the time between instruction and actionable data helps teachers identify which students need support faster. Real-time assessment results, differentiated activity banks, and built-in accommodation tools align directly with RTI's core requirements.
- **P:** Wayground first-person signal: "Wayground's formative assessment activities and real-time analytics give teachers the data they need to identify students who aren't responding to Tier 1 instruction, with differentiated versions of any activity available in seconds." (natural, non-promotional — 2nd or 3rd Wayground mention)
- **E:** Practical examples: use an activity as a Tier 1 formative check at the end of a lesson; use differentiated versions for Tier 2 small groups; use read-aloud + translation accommodations for ELL students at any tier. Note that Wayground's 25+ accommodations (free for all US educators) directly support Tier 2 and Tier 3 modifications.
