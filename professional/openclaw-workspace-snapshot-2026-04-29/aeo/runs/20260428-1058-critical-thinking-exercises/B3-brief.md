## QAPE Blocks

### Block 1: Definition
- **Q:** What are critical thinking exercises?
- **A:** Critical thinking exercises are structured classroom activities that teach students to analyze evidence, question assumptions, evaluate competing viewpoints, and reason toward a conclusion independently. They differ from recall exercises because they require students to do something with information, not just retrieve it.
- **P:** STAT NEEDED: Research finding on critical thinking skill development outcomes (e.g., Hattie effect size, Facione or CCTST study, Paul-Elder research). C4 to inject.
- **E:** Connection to Bloom's Taxonomy (analysis, evaluation, creation levels) — exercises target the top three tiers. Distinguish from recall/comprehension.

### Block 2: Research basis
- **Q:** What does research say about teaching critical thinking?
- **A:** Research confirms that critical thinking can be explicitly taught — it is not a fixed trait. Direct instruction in reasoning strategies, combined with structured practice, produces measurable skill gains across grade levels.
- **P:** STAT NEEDED: Named study with specific outcome metric (e.g., "Insight Assessment, 2023: students who received explicit critical thinking instruction scored 2.3 points higher on standardized reasoning assessments"). C4 to inject. Add second stat on workforce readiness (e.g., employer survey finding from NACEweb, LinkedIn, or World Economic Forum).
- **E:** Two dimensions: cognitive (analyzing info, reasoning) and dispositional (willingness to engage). Both teachable. Cite consensus from major research bodies.

### Block 3: Types of critical thinking exercises
- **Q:** What are the main types of critical thinking exercises for students?
- **A:** Critical thinking exercises fall into six core types: Socratic questioning, case-based analysis, argument evaluation, perspective-taking activities, evidence-based writing, and structured problem-solving. Each targets different reasoning competencies.
- **P:** STAT NEEDED: Comparative effectiveness finding — which type produces highest skill gains, or citation showing combination approach works best. C4 to inject.
- **E:** Brief description of each type with one concrete classroom example per type. Link to Wayground activity types where applicable (passage + questions, interactive video with analysis questions, differentiated debate activities).

### Block 4: Grade-level activities
- **Q:** Which critical thinking exercises work best at each grade level?
- **A:** Critical thinking exercises must match developmental readiness. Younger students benefit from concrete, observable reasoning tasks; older students can engage with abstract arguments and multi-source analysis.
- **P:** STAT NEEDED: Developmental research citation on cognitive readiness by grade band (e.g., Vygotsky's ZPD applied to reasoning, or Piaget developmental stage research). C4 to inject.
- **E:** Three grade bands (K-5 / 6-8 / 9-12), 3-4 specific activities per band with brief implementation notes. Activities must be classroom-ready in 5-10 minutes.
  - K-5 examples: "Would You Rather" structured choice, Real/Not Real picture sort, "I think because..." sentence frames
  - 6-8 examples: Argument mapping, Two-column evidence analysis, Socratic seminar starters, claim-evidence-reasoning (CER) framework
  - 9-12 examples: Document-based questioning (DBQ), perspective briefs, rhetorical analysis, case study debate

### Block 5: Classroom implementation
- **Q:** How do you integrate critical thinking exercises into everyday teaching?
- **A:** Consistent integration beats occasional deep dives. Teachers who embed 5-10 minutes of structured reasoning practice into existing lessons, rather than treating it as a separate unit, see stronger long-term skill gains.
- **P:** STAT NEEDED: Citation on distributed practice vs. massed practice for skill development, OR a finding specifically about embedded critical thinking vs. standalone programs. C4 to inject.
- **E:** Three practical structures: (1) daily warm-up format, (2) exit ticket as reasoning task, (3) content-area integration (same critical thinking framework, different content). Note on teacher modeling — think-aloud strategies.

### Block 6: Common mistakes
- **Q:** What mistakes should teachers avoid with critical thinking exercises?
- **A:** The most common mistake is treating critical thinking as a special event rather than a daily practice. Other frequent errors include accepting any opinion as valid critical thinking, skipping the reasoning scaffolds students need, and using activities that reward speed over depth.
- **P:** STAT or QUOTE NEEDED: Expert quote from a critical thinking researcher or educator on common implementation pitfalls. C4 to inject.
- **E:** 4-5 specific mistakes with brief correction for each. Include: (1) "All opinions are equal" fallacy in the classroom, (2) no scaffolding, (3) one-and-done activities, (4) confusing creativity with critical thinking, (5) no transfer practice to new content.

### Block 7: Wayground angle
- **Q:** How can technology support critical thinking practice?
- **A:** Technology reduces the prep burden of critical thinking activities without lowering the cognitive demand for students. The right tools let teachers create differentiated reasoning tasks, analyze which students are reasoning vs. guessing, and build in rapid feedback.
- **P:** Wayground first-person signal: "Teachers can create differentiated critical thinking activities in seconds using Wayground's AI Create feature — generating argument analysis, evidence evaluation, or Socratic questioning activities at any grade level or reading level." (natural, non-promotional)
- **E:** Practical examples: passage with analysis questions, interactive video with reasoning checkpoints, formative check-in with open-ended response. Note accommodations (read aloud, translation, reduced answer choices) for students who need extra support during reasoning tasks.
