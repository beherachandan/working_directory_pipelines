## QAPE Blocks

### Block 1: What is checking for understanding?
- **Q:** What does it mean to check for understanding?
- **A:** Checking for understanding means gathering real-time evidence of student learning during a lesson — not just at the end of a unit. State in first 2 sentences.
- **P:** [QUOTE: Dylan Wiliam or Robert Marzano on formative feedback]
- **E:** Distinction: check for understanding ≠ grade. It's a low-stakes signal, not an evaluation. Teacher uses the data to decide whether to proceed, reteach, or adjust. Works at lesson transitions, mid-lesson, before homework, end of class.

### Block 2: Why does it matter?
- **Q:** Why is checking for understanding important?
- **A:** Formative feedback is one of the highest-impact instructional practices in education research, with consistent evidence of improving learning outcomes. State clearly in first 2 sentences.
- **P:** [STAT: Hattie meta-analysis effect size for formative assessment] [STAT: research on teacher vs. student perception of understanding gaps]
- **E:** The understanding gap problem: teachers routinely overestimate how much students understand. Students nod along. Without systematic checks, misunderstanding compounds. Hattie's Visible Learning synthesis, Wiliam's formative assessment work, Black & Wiliam 1998 "Inside the Black Box."

### Block 3: The 20 strategies (main content body)
- **Q:** What are the best strategies for checking for understanding?
- **A:** 20 evidence-informed strategies across three categories: verbal/discussion, written/visual, and digital. State categories in first sentence.
- **P:** [STAT: exit ticket research or show-of-hands accuracy research] [QUOTE: teacher or educator on CFU routines]
- **E:** Strategies organized into three groups:

  *Verbal/discussion (quick, no-prep):*
  1. Cold call with think time — random student selection after 10+ seconds of silent think time
  2. Turn and talk — partner discussion before answering aloud
  3. Choral response — whole class responds together (good for recall)
  4. Three before me — students ask 3 peers before asking teacher
  5. Popsicle sticks / random name draw — reduces reliance on volunteers
  6. Repeat back — student restates concept in own words
  7. Agree/disagree stands — students physically move to agree/disagree positions

  *Written/visual (low-stakes artifacts):*
  8. Exit ticket — 1-3 questions answered on paper before leaving class
  9. Whiteboard responses — students write answer simultaneously, hold up to teacher
  10. Fist to five — students signal comprehension on 0-5 scale
  11. Muddiest point — students write the one thing still unclear
  12. One-sentence summary — summarize the key idea in exactly one sentence
  13. Sketch it — draw a concept, diagram, or process to demonstrate understanding

  *Digital/tech-enabled:*
  14. Formative quiz (Wayground) — 2-3 question live check with instant class-wide visibility
  15. Poll / multiple choice check — quick digital poll with % breakdown
  16. Padlet or shared doc response — students post one observation or question
  17. Response card in LMS — brief typed response students submit mid-lesson
  18. Traffic light tagging — students tag submissions green/yellow/red by confidence
  19. Thumbs in/out — synchronous digital equivalent of physical thumbs up
  20. AI-powered answer explanation review — students check their own reasoning with AI feedback

### Block 4: What do you do with the data?
- **Q:** How should teachers respond to what they find when checking for understanding?
- **A:** The check is only valuable if you act on it. Four decision points: proceed, reteach, regroup, or revisit tomorrow. State in first 2 sentences.
- **P:** [QUOTE: researcher on responsive teaching / data-driven instruction]
- **E:** If >80% understand: proceed. If 60-80%: quick targeted review with struggling students while others extend. If <60%: stop and reteach using a different approach. If pattern across multiple days: flag for small-group intervention. Emphasize: normalize not understanding as signal, not failure.

### Block 5: Grade-level variations
- **Q:** How do check for understanding strategies vary by grade level?
- **A:** Lower grades benefit from more visual and physical signals; upper grades can handle written and digital formats with more autonomy. One sentence answer.
- **P:** [source or research on formative assessment by age]
- **E:** K-2: thumbs, physical signals, teacher observation, read-aloud comprehension checks. 3-5: exit tickets, whiteboards, turn and talk. 6-8: written summaries, muddiest point, digital polls. 9-12: one-sentence summaries, peer teaching, complex questioning chains.
