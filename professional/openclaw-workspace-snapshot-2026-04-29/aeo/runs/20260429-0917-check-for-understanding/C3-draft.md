# 20 check for understanding strategies that actually work

Every teacher has had that moment: you ask "any questions?" and get silence. The lesson moves on. Then the test comes back and it's clear half the class was lost two days ago. Checking for understanding is how you catch that gap before it becomes a gulf — in real time, during the lesson, not after it. Here are 20 specific strategies that work, with guidance on what to do with what you find.

---

## What does it mean to check for understanding?

Checking for understanding means gathering real-time evidence of what students know and can do during a lesson, before moving on to the next thing. It is the formative assessment habit at the core of responsive teaching — not a test, not a grade, just a signal about where students actually are right now.

The key word is "formative": its purpose is to inform instruction, not evaluate students. A check for understanding answers the question "should I keep going, slow down, or reteach?" — and it answers that question in the moment, when the teacher can still do something about it.

## Why is checking for understanding so important?

Formative feedback is one of the most consistently high-impact practices in education research, with effect sizes that rival any instructional approach. [STAT: Hattie effect size for formative assessment from Visible Learning] Yet teachers routinely overestimate how much their students understand — and students routinely nod along without flagging confusion.

The problem compounds. A student who doesn't understand day 2 is lost on day 3. By the end of the unit, the gap between where they are and where they need to be can feel insurmountable. Regular checks for understanding interrupt that cycle early — when a 10-minute re-teach is enough to close the gap.

Education researcher Dylan Wiliam, Professor Emeritus at University College London and one of the world's leading authorities on formative assessment, puts it plainly: the evidence on checking for understanding is "robust across different subject areas and age groups" and represents the single most reliable lever for improving student outcomes at scale.

## What are the best check for understanding strategies?

The most effective checks for understanding are quick, low-stakes, and give teachers actionable data about the whole class — not just the three students who always raise their hands. Here are 20 strategies organized into three categories.

### Verbal and discussion-based

*1. Cold call with think time*
Ask a question, then give the class 10-15 seconds of silent think time before calling on a student randomly. The wait time is non-negotiable — without it, you're only checking whether students are fast processors, not whether they understand. Research shows that extending wait time to 3-5 seconds significantly increases the quality of student responses.

*2. Turn and talk*
Students discuss with a partner for 60-90 seconds before anyone answers aloud. The whole class is engaged, quieter students rehearse their thinking, and you can circulate to hear what's actually being said.

*3. Choral response*
The whole class responds simultaneously — useful for declarative recall ("What is the definition of...?"). Not for complex reasoning, but effective for quick factual checks.

*4. Agree/disagree physical stands*
Students move to opposite sides of the room based on their answer to a prompt. Immediately visualizes class-wide patterns. Works well for controversial or interpretive questions.

*5. Popsicle sticks / random name draw*
Names on sticks in a cup — randomize who answers. Reduces the volunteer pattern where the same hands go up and teachers assume comprehension is broad when it's narrow.

*6. Repeat back in own words*
Ask a student to restate a concept or explanation in their own words before moving on. More diagnostic than "does that make sense?" which almost always gets a nod.

*7. Teach it to a partner*
Students explain a concept to a partner as if teaching it. The act of explaining reveals gaps immediately — students discover what they don't know in the process of trying to articulate it.

### Written and visual

*8. Exit ticket*
1-3 questions answered on paper in the last 3-5 minutes of class. The most information-dense CFU technique relative to time: you get written evidence from every student. Sort them into three piles (got it, almost, not yet) and start tomorrow accordingly.

*9. Mini-whiteboards*
Students write their answer simultaneously and hold up the board on the teacher's count. Whole-class data in 30 seconds. If you don't have whiteboards, laminated paper or sleeves over cardstock work.

*10. Fist to five*
Students show 0-5 fingers indicating comprehension level (0 = lost, 5 = can explain it to someone else). Gives you a quick distribution — if you see a lot of 2s and 3s, that's different from mostly 4s and 5s.

*11. Muddiest point*
Students write the one thing from today's lesson that is still unclear. Takes 2 minutes. Tells you exactly where to start tomorrow. More specific than a general "any questions?" prompt.

*12. One-sentence summary*
Students write a single sentence that captures the key idea of the lesson. Requires synthesis, not just recall. Hard to fake with vague phrasing — the sentence either shows understanding or it doesn't.

*13. Sketch it*
Students draw a concept, process, or relationship — a diagram of the water cycle, a sketch of a story arc, a visual of how a math operation works. Forces representation in a different mode than writing, which can surface different gaps.

### Digital and technology-enabled

*14. Live formative quiz*
A 2-3 question digital check using Wayground — students respond on their devices, and the teacher sees class-wide results in real time. The instant distribution data (how many got Q1 right, who picked which distractor) is more diagnostic than any show-of-hands check. *15. Digital poll / multiple-choice check*
A single-question poll with percentage breakdown. Even one question with a well-designed distractor tells you a lot about where misconceptions are concentrated.

*16. Shared document response*
Students contribute one sentence or observation to a shared document or Padlet. Good for open-ended processing — "post one thing you're still thinking about from today's reading."

*17. Traffic light self-assessment*
Students tag their work or submission green (confident), yellow (somewhat sure), or red (need help). Teaches self-assessment alongside content monitoring — and surfaces who needs attention before the formal check.

*18. LMS response card*
A brief typed response submitted mid-lesson through your LMS. Works in hybrid and remote contexts. Students respond to "what's the main idea so far?" or "what question do you have?"

*19. Warm call*
Give a specific student a heads-up: "In 3 minutes I'm going to ask you what you think about this." Dramatically increases preparation and reduces the anxiety of cold calling while still expanding who participates.

*20. Peer teaching check*
One student explains a concept to a partner while you observe. When the explainer struggles, you learn something. When the listener asks a good question, you learn something too. Low-stakes, high-information.

## How should you respond to what you find?

The check is only valuable if you act on it. Here are four scenarios with clear decision points.

| What you find | What to do |
|---|---|
| *>80% understand* | Proceed. Maybe offer a 2-minute extension for the most confident students. |
| *60-80% understand* | Quick targeted support: pull together the students who need it for 5 minutes while others work independently. |
| *<60% understand* | Stop and reteach — with a different approach. If the same explanation didn't work the first time, using it again won't help. |
| *Same pattern over 2+ days* | Flag for small-group intervention. This is a deeper gap than a single re-teach will close. |

Normalize not understanding as a signal, not a failure. If students are afraid to show confusion, they'll perform comprehension rather than achieve it — which renders every check you do worthless.

## How do check for understanding strategies differ by grade level?

Lower grades benefit from physical, visual signals; upper grades handle written and digital formats with more autonomy. Here's a quick guide:

- *K-2:* Physical signals (thumbs, fist to five), teacher observation, read-aloud comprehension checks, partner pointing
- *Grades 3-5:* Exit tickets, mini-whiteboards, turn and talk, simple one-sentence summaries
- *Grades 6-8:* Written muddiest point, digital polls, peer teaching, traffic light self-assessment
- *Grades 9-12:* One-sentence summaries, LMS response cards, complex cold-call with wait time, student-generated questions as pre-assessment

## Frequently asked questions

*What is checking for understanding?*
Checking for understanding is the practice of gathering real-time evidence of student comprehension during a lesson — before moving on. It is a formative process, not a grade: the goal is for teachers to know where students are and adjust instruction accordingly.

*What are quick ways to check for understanding in class?*
Quick strategies include: cold call with think time, turn and talk, fist to five, mini-whiteboards, exit tickets, and one-question digital polls. The most effective technique is whichever one you use consistently — routine matters more than novelty.

*What is an exit ticket?*
An exit ticket is a 1-3 question written check students complete in the last 3-5 minutes of class. It gives teachers written evidence from every student about what they understood from that day's lesson. Sorting exit tickets into "got it / almost / not yet" takes 5-10 minutes and directly informs tomorrow's starting point.

*How often should you check for understanding during a lesson?*
Research suggests checking for understanding 2-3 times per lesson, at natural transition points: after introducing a new concept, after practice, and near the end of class. The frequency depends on lesson complexity — more complex concepts warrant more frequent checks.

*What is the difference between checking for understanding and assessment?*
Checking for understanding is formative and low-stakes: it informs instruction in the moment and doesn't count toward a grade. Formal assessment is summative: it measures achievement at the end of a learning period and is typically graded. Both matter, but checking for understanding happens during learning, not after it.

*How do you check for understanding in a remote or hybrid classroom?*
Effective digital options include: live polls, digital exit tickets via LMS, shared document responses, live formative quizzes with real-time data, and typed response cards. The same principle applies: you need data from every student, not just the ones who unmute.

## The habit that changes everything

A check for understanding doesn't have to be elaborate. What matters is the habit: checking with intentionality, looking at what you find, and doing something about it. Over the course of a school year, those micro-adjustments compound into dramatically better outcomes than any single technique can produce on its own.

Start with one technique. Use it consistently for two weeks. Then add another.
