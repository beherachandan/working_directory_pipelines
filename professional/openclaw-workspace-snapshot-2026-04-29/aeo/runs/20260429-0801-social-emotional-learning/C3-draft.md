# What is social emotional learning? The teacher's guide to SEL

Teachers already know what social emotional learning is — they've been doing it for years. Managing conflict between students, coaching a child through frustration, building classroom norms that let everyone feel safe enough to try and fail. That's SEL. What the field has done is name it, research it, and give teachers a framework for doing it more intentionally. Here's what the research shows and what it looks like in practice.

---

## What is social emotional learning (SEL)?

Social emotional learning (SEL) is the process through which students develop and practice the skills to understand and manage their emotions, build positive relationships, and make responsible decisions. It is grounded in the CASEL framework, which defines five core competencies that form the basis for healthy development across school, family, and community settings.

SEL is not a separate subject — it is embedded in classroom culture, daily routines, and how teachers and students interact. It is not therapy or crisis intervention, though it complements both.

## What are the 5 CASEL competencies?

The CASEL framework defines five SEL competencies that together describe the full range of social and emotional development. These are the skills students need to learn and life outcomes schools are trying to build.

- *Self-awareness* — the ability to recognize one's own emotions, thoughts, and values, and understand how they influence behavior. In the classroom: a student who notices "I'm frustrated right now and that's making it harder to focus."

- *Self-management* — the ability to regulate emotions, manage stress, control impulses, and work toward goals. In the classroom: a student who uses a breathing strategy before a test, or who persists through a difficult problem rather than giving up.

- *Social awareness* — the ability to understand the perspectives of others and empathize, even across difference. In the classroom: a student who listens to a peer's reasoning with genuine curiosity rather than waiting for their turn to talk.

- *Relationship skills* — the ability to communicate clearly, cooperate, negotiate conflict, and build and maintain positive relationships. In the classroom: a student who helps a group reach consensus on a project without dominating or disengaging.

- *Responsible decision-making* — the ability to make ethical, constructive choices by considering the well-being of oneself and others. In the classroom: a student who thinks through consequences before acting, even under social pressure.

## Why is social emotional learning important?

Students who receive evidence-based SEL instruction show measurable improvements in academic performance, reduced behavioral problems, and better long-term life outcomes — making SEL one of the highest-ROI investments a school can make.

The landmark evidence comes from a meta-analysis by Durlak, Weissberg, and colleagues (2011) that reviewed 213 school-based SEL programs: students in SEL programs outperformed control groups by 11 percentile points in academic achievement and showed a 24% improvement in positive social behaviors. [CITATION NEEDED — Durlak et al. 2011 primary citation]

The "soft skills" framing understates the case. SEL outcomes are quantifiable:

- Improved academic achievement (11-percentile-point gain across studies)
- Reduced behavioral problems and disciplinary incidents
- Improved mental health and reduced anxiety and depression symptoms
- Better attendance
- Long-term effects including higher graduation rates and improved employment outcomes

The research effect is particularly strong for students from underserved communities, where SEL programs that explicitly build self-management and relationship skills show significant reductions in the opportunity gap.

## How do you incorporate SEL into your classroom?

SEL doesn't require a separate curriculum — it's embedded in how you start class, how you give feedback, how you structure group work, and how you respond to student behavior. The most effective SEL instruction is integrated, not isolated.

[CITATION NEEDED — research stat or expert quote on integrated vs isolated SEL instruction outcomes]

Daily-routine strategies that embed SEL naturally:

- *Morning meeting or structured check-in* — a brief (5-minute) daily ritual where students share how they're feeling or set an intention for the day. Builds self-awareness and belonging simultaneously.
- *Emotion vocabulary building* — explicitly teach students words for emotions beyond "good/bad/fine." Students who can name what they're feeling can manage it more effectively.
- *Reflective journaling prompts* — brief end-of-class or end-of-week prompts ("What did I find hard today?" "What am I proud of?") build metacognition and self-awareness with minimal time.
- *Collaborative problem-solving protocols* — structured routines for group disagreements (e.g., identify the problem, generate options, agree on a solution) build relationship skills through repeated practice.
- *Restorative practices* — when conflict or behavior issues arise, restorative conversations ("what happened, who was affected, how do we repair it") build accountability and relationship skills more effectively than punitive responses alone.

## Social emotional learning activities that actually work

The most effective SEL activities build specific competencies through structured practice — they give students a safe, low-stakes space to develop self-awareness, practice empathy, and navigate social challenges.

Activities by competency:

- *Emotion check-ins* (self-awareness) — students indicate their emotional state at the start of class using a visual scale, color system, or brief sentence. Takes 2-3 minutes, generates real data about the room.
- *Feeling journals* (self-awareness, self-management) — dedicated journals for daily or weekly reflective prompts. Students write privately; some choose to share. Builds introspective habit over time.
- *Perspective-taking discussions* (social awareness) — structured discussions around a scenario or text where students are asked to identify the perspective of a character different from themselves.
- *Collaborative challenges* (relationship skills) — structured group tasks with defined roles and explicit norms around communication. Debrief the process ("how did your group make decisions?") as much as the content.
- *Circle time* (multiple competencies) — community circles where students share in a structured format (restorative, check-in, or academic) build trust and belonging over the school year.
- *Mindfulness minutes* (self-management) — brief guided breathing or grounding exercises before assessments or transitions. Research shows even brief mindfulness practices reduce cortisol and improve focus. [CITATION NEEDED]
- *Goal-setting and tracking* (self-management, responsible decision-making) — students set short-term learning or behavior goals and track progress. Weekly or biweekly check-ins. Builds agency.
- *Role-play scenarios* (relationship skills, responsible decision-making) — structured scenarios where students practice navigating conflict, peer pressure, or ethical dilemmas. Most effective with explicit debrief.

## SEL across grade levels

SEL competencies develop progressively. What's appropriate for kindergarteners looks different from what's appropriate for high schoolers — but the underlying framework stays the same.

[CITATION NEEDED — CASEL grade-band framework or research on developmental progression of SEL competencies]

*Elementary (K-5)*
Primary SEL goals: naming and managing emotions, developing empathy, building basic cooperation skills. Activities: feelings charts, partner sharing routines, classroom job systems that build responsibility, read-aloud discussions about character emotions, movement breaks with self-regulation focus.

*Middle school (6-8)*
Primary SEL goals: managing more complex emotions (peer pressure, identity, belonging), developing perspective-taking across difference, building resilience. Activities: journaling, collaborative projects with explicit relationship-skill debrief, restorative circles, direct instruction on stress management strategies.

*High school (9-12)*
Primary SEL goals: responsible decision-making in complex situations, maintaining healthy relationships, preparing for adult autonomy. Activities: case study discussions of ethical dilemmas, student-led seminars, goal-setting with authentic stakes, community service or project-based learning with real community impact.

## What research shows about SEL outcomes

The evidence base for SEL is among the strongest in education. A landmark meta-analysis by Durlak, Weissberg, and colleagues (2011) of 213 SEL programs found students in SEL programs outperformed controls by 11 percentile points academically and showed a 24% improvement in positive social behaviors. [CITATION NEEDED — Durlak et al. 2011 full citation + CASEL 2023 effectiveness review]

Outcome data across well-studied SEL programs consistently shows:

- Academic achievement gains of 11-25 percentile points in well-implemented programs
- 24% reduction in behavioral problems
- 20% improvement in school attendance in some studies
- Significant reductions in anxiety, depression, and substance use in longitudinal studies
- Long-term effects including higher graduation rates, better employment outcomes, and reduced involvement in the criminal justice system

The evidence is clear enough that many states and districts now require SEL standards — Ohio, Illinois, Kansas City, and dozens of others have developed explicit SEL frameworks that parallel academic standards in scope and implementation.

## Frequently asked questions

*What is social emotional learning?*
Social emotional learning (SEL) is the process through which students develop skills to understand and manage emotions, build positive relationships, and make responsible decisions. It is grounded in the CASEL framework and includes five core competencies: self-awareness, self-management, social awareness, relationship skills, and responsible decision-making.

*What are the 5 CASEL competencies?*
The five CASEL SEL competencies are self-awareness, self-management, social awareness, relationship skills, and responsible decision-making. Each describes a domain of social and emotional development that research shows is critical for academic success and long-term well-being.

*Why is SEL important in schools?*
SEL is important because students who develop social and emotional skills show measurable improvements in academic performance, behavior, and mental health. A 2011 meta-analysis of 213 SEL programs found students outperformed controls by 11 percentile points academically and showed a 24% improvement in positive social behaviors.

*How do you teach social emotional learning?*
The most effective SEL teaching is integrated into daily classroom routines — check-ins, reflective journaling, collaborative protocols, and restorative practices — rather than delivered as a separate subject. Explicit instruction in SEL skills (naming emotions, conflict resolution steps, goal-setting) is most effective when combined with authentic practice opportunities.

*What are some social emotional learning activities?*
Effective SEL activities include morning check-ins, emotion journals, perspective-taking discussions, collaborative challenges with debrief, mindfulness minutes, circle time, role-play scenarios, and goal-setting routines. The most effective activities target specific CASEL competencies and include time for reflection.

*Does social emotional learning improve academic performance?*

*How do you assess social emotional learning?*
SEL assessment looks different from academic testing. Common approaches include teacher observation using structured rubrics, student self-assessment surveys, behavior data tracking, and climate surveys that measure belonging and safety. ## SEL is already in your classroom

Every time you pause to help a student name what they're feeling, every time you design group work with roles and norms, every time you respond to behavior with curiosity before correction — you're doing SEL.

The research gives it a name and a framework. What it shows is that doing it intentionally, consistently, and explicitly makes a real difference in outcomes that matter: academic achievement, behavior, mental health, and how students feel about school.

The simplest place to start: five minutes at the beginning of class, asking how students are actually doing. The compound effect of that conversation, repeated every day, is what SEL is made of.
